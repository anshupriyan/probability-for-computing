# 05. Bayes' Formula and Law of Total Probability (Section 1.6)

Imagine you are looking at a footprint at a crime scene. You want to work backward from this evidence to figure out who the suspect is. 

In mathematics, this "backwards" reasoning is governed by **Bayes' Formula**, which relies on the **Law of Total Probability**.

---

## 1. Law of Total Probability: The "Weighted Paths" Map

Suppose you want to travel to a city (Event $E$). There are different routes you can take. First, you must choose a route (e.g. Route $F$, or Route $F^c$). Once you choose a route, you have a certain probability of arriving safely.

We can write the total probability of arriving ($P(E)$) as the sum of the probabilities of arriving via each route:

```
            ┌── Route F  (Prob: P(F))   ──> Arrive (Prob: P(E|F))   ──> Path Prob: P(F)P(E|F)
── Start ───┤
            └── Route Fᶜ (Prob: P(Fᶜ))  ──> Arrive (Prob: P(E|Fᶜ))  ──> Path Prob: P(Fᶜ)P(E|Fᶜ)
```

Adding these paths together gives the **Law of Total Probability**:
$$P(E) = P(E|F)P(F) + P(E|F^c)P(F^c)$$

### General Partition
If the sample space $S$ is divided into $n$ separate, non-overlapping routes $F_1, F_2, \dots, F_n$ (which partition the space):
$$P(E) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(E|F_i)P(F_i)$$

---

## 2. Bayes' Formula: The "Detective's Clue"

> 💡 **The Analogy: Working Backwards**
> A detective arrives at a crime scene and sees a footprint (Event $E$ has occurred). 
> - The suspect could be Person $F_1$, Person $F_2$, or Person $F_3$ (the partition).
> - The detective knows how likely each person was to be at the scene beforehand (the **prior probability** $P(F_j)$).
> - The detective also knows how likely each person is to wear shoes matching the footprint (the **likelihood** $P(E|F_j)$).
> - The detective wants to find: **Given the footprint, what is the probability that Person $F_j$ is the suspect?** (the **posterior probability** $P(F_j | E)$).

By definition of conditional probability:
$$P(F_j | E) = \frac{P(E F_j)}{P(E)}$$
Using the multiplication rule for the numerator and the Law of Total Probability for the denominator, we get **Bayes' Formula**:
$$P(F_j | E) = \frac{P(E|F_j)P(F_j)}{\sum_{i=1}^n P(E|F_i)P(F_i)}$$

## 3. The Diagnostics Paradox (Example 1.14)

* **Problem**: A laboratory blood test is 95% effective in detecting a disease when present. It yields a 1% false positive rate for healthy people. If 0.5% of the population actually has the disease, what is the probability a person has the disease given they tested positive?
* **First-Time Trap**: Most people guess around 90-95% since the test is "95% effective". Let's walk through the math to see why this is a massive error.
* **Concept Breakdown**:
  - Let $D$ be the event the person has the disease: $P(D) = 0.005$ (0.5% prevalence).
  - Let $D^c$ be the event the person is healthy: $P(D^c) = 0.995$.
  - Let $E$ be the event the test is positive.
    - True positive rate: $P(E|D) = 0.95$.
    - False positive rate: $P(E|D^c) = 0.01$.
  - We want to find $P(D|E)$ (probability of having the disease given a positive test).
  - Apply Bayes' formula:
    $$P(D|E) = \frac{P(E|D)P(D)}{P(E|D)P(D) + P(E|D^c)P(D^c)}$$
    $$P(D|E) = \frac{(0.95)(0.005)}{(0.95)(0.005) + (0.01)(0.995)} = \frac{0.00475}{0.00475 + 0.00995} = \frac{0.00475}{0.0147} = \frac{95}{294} \approx 0.323$$
* **Conclusion**: There is only a **32.3% chance** that a person who tests positive actually has the disease! 
* **Intuition**: 
  - Imagine a group of 10,000 people. 
  - Only 50 people have the disease (0.5% of 10,000). Out of these 50, the test will correctly catch 95%, which is about **48 positive tests**.
  - 9,950 people are healthy. Out of these, the test will falsely flag 1% as positive, which is about **100 false positive tests**.
  - Total positive tests = $48 + 100 = 148$.
  - Out of those 148 positive tests, only 48 actually have the disease: $\frac{48}{148} \approx 32.4\%$. 
  - Because the disease is so rare, the number of false positives from the large healthy population dwarfs the number of true positives from the small sick population!

## 4. The "Missing Letter" Search Problem (Example 1.15)

* **Problem**: A letter is equally likely to be in folder 1, 2, or 3 ($P(F_i) = 1/3$). Let $\alpha_i$ be the probability that you will find the letter when searching folder $i$, if it is actually in folder $i$. You search folder 1 and do not find it. What is the probability that the letter is in folder 1?
* **Walkthrough**:
  - Let $F_i$ be the event that the letter is in folder $i$ ($P(F_i) = \frac{1}{3}$).
  - Let $E$ be the event that a search of folder 1 does not find the letter. We want to find $P(F_1 | E)$.
  - Find the conditional probabilities of missing the letter:
    - $P(E|F_1) = 1 - \alpha_1$ (it is in folder 1, but we missed it)
    - $P(E|F_2) = 1$ (it is in folder 2, so searching folder 1 will definitely not find it)
    - $P(E|F_3) = 1$ (it is in folder 3, so searching folder 1 will definitely not find it)
  - Applying Bayes' formula:
    $$P(F_1 | E) = \frac{P(E|F_1)P(F_1)}{P(E|F_1)P(F_1) + P(E|F_2)P(F_2) + P(E|F_3)P(F_3)}$$
    $$P(F_1 | E) = \frac{(1 - \alpha_1) \cdot \frac{1}{3}}{(1 - \alpha_1) \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{3}} = \frac{1 - \alpha_1}{1 - \alpha_1 + 2} = \frac{1 - \alpha_1}{3 - \alpha_1}$$
  - *Intuition*: If the search was perfect ($\alpha_1 = 1$), then $P(F_1 | E) = 0$ (if you didn't find it in a perfect search, it's definitely not there). If the search was useless ($\alpha_1 = 0$), then $P(F_1 | E) = \frac{1}{3}$ (you learned nothing).
