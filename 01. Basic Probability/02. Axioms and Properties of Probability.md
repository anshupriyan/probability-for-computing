# 02. Axioms and Properties of Probability (Section 1.3)

Once we have our "Menu of Outcomes" (Sample Space $S$) and our "Sub-lists" (Events $E$), we need to assign numbers to these lists to represent their likelihood. 

To ensure our math remains consistent, we must follow three simple rules: the **Axioms of Probability**.

---

## 1. The Core Analogy: The "100% Budget Limit"

> 💡 **Mental Model**: Think of probability as a **Budget** of exactly **1.0** (or $100\%$). 
> You can distribute this budget among different events, but you must follow three rules:
> 1. You cannot have a negative budget, and you cannot exceed 100%. (Axiom i)
> 2. The entire menu of possibilities has a budget of exactly 100%. (Axiom ii)
> 3. If you buy a cookie and a sandwich, the total cost is the sum of their individual costs, provided they don't overlap. (Axiom iii)

### The Three Axioms Formally:

### Axiom (i)
For any event $E$:
$$0 \le P(E) \le 1$$
* **Why**: Probability can never be negative, and it cannot exceed 1 (or 100%).

### Axiom (ii)
The probability of the entire sample space $S$ is 1:
$$P(S) = 1$$
* **Why**: Something *must* happen when you run the experiment. The probability that the outcome lies somewhere on the menu is 100%.

### Axiom (iii)
For any sequence of mutually exclusive events $E_1, E_2, \dots$ (disjoint events where $E_n E_m = \varnothing$ when $n \ne m$):
$$P\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty E_n\right) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty P(E_n)$$
* **Why**: If events don't overlap, the probability of *either* of them happening is simply the sum of their individual probabilities.

---

## 2. Fundamental Properties & Step-by-Step Proofs

Using these three axioms, we can prove standard probability formulas. Let's walk through the math.

### Property 1: The Probability of the Complement
The probability that an event $E$ does **not** occur is:
$$P(E^c) = 1 - P(E)$$

#### 📝 Step-by-step Proof:
1. By definition, $E$ and $E^c$ (not $E$) have no overlap, meaning they are mutually exclusive:
   $$E E^c = \varnothing$$
2. Together, they cover every possible outcome in the sample space:
   $$E \cup E^c = S$$
3. Apply Axiom (ii) ($P(S) = 1$) to their union:
   $$P(E \cup E^c) = P(S) = 1$$
4. Since $E$ and $E^c$ are mutually exclusive, apply Axiom (iii) to split the union:
   $$P(E) + P(E^c) = 1$$
5. Subtract $P(E)$ from both sides:
   $$P(E^c) = 1 - P(E) \quad \blacksquare$$
* *Side Note*: If $E = S$, then $P(S^c) = P(\varnothing) = 1 - P(S) = 1 - 1 = 0$. The probability of nothing happening is exactly 0.

### Property 2: The Union of Overlapping Events
For any two events $E$ and $F$ (which may or may not overlap):
$$P(E \cup F) = P(E) + P(F) - P(EF)$$

> 💡 **The Analogy: Double-Counting the Overlap**
> Imagine you are counting students in a classroom. Let $E$ be the set of chocolate lovers and $F$ be the set of vanilla lovers.
> - If you count all chocolate lovers ($P(E)$) and add all vanilla lovers ($P(F)$), you have **double-counted** the students who like **both** ($P(EF)$).
> - To get the true total of students who like chocolate *or* vanilla, you must subtract the overlap once to correct the count.

```
      E (Chocolate)             F (Vanilla)
   ┌─────────────────┐       ┌─────────────────┐
   │                 │  EF   │                 │
   │    Only E       │ ┌───┐ │     Only F      │
   │                 │ │   │ │                 │
   └─────────────────┘ └───┘ └─────────────────┘
                        Double Counted!
```

#### 📝 Step-by-step Proof:
1. We can break the union $E \cup F$ into two disjoint events: $E$ and $E^c F$ (outcomes in $F$ that are not in $E$).
   $$E \cup F = E \cup (E^c F)$$
2. Since these are mutually exclusive, apply Axiom (iii):
   $$P(E \cup F) = P(E) + P(E^c F)$$
3. Similarly, we can write the event $F$ as the union of two disjoint events: $EF$ (in both) and $E^c F$ (in $F$ but not $E$).
   $$F = EF \cup (E^c F)$$
4. Apply Axiom (iii) to this representation of $F$:
   $$P(F) = P(EF) + P(E^c F) \implies P(E^c F) = P(F) - P(EF)$$
5. Substitute this expression for $P(E^c F)$ back into our union formula:
   $$P(E \cup F) = P(E) + P(F) - P(EF) \quad \blacksquare$$

---

## 3. Inclusion-Exclusion for Multiple Events

What if we have three events ($E, F, G$)? 
The same "double-counting" logic applies, but with an extra layer:
$$P(E \cup F \cup G) = P(E) + P(F) + P(G) - P(EF) - P(EG) - P(FG) + P(EFG)$$

#### 🔍 Why do we add the triple intersection $P(EFG)$ at the end?
1. Adding $P(E) + P(F) + P(G)$ counts the central intersection $EFG$ three times.
2. Subtracting the pairs $- P(EF) - P(EG) - P(FG)$ subtracts that central intersection three times.
3. Now, the center $EFG$ is counted $3 - 3 = 0$ times! We have completely lost it.
4. To fix this, we must add $P(EFG)$ back once.
