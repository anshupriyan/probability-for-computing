# 01. Sample Space and Events (Section 1.1 & 1.2)

To study probability, we need a mathematical language to describe random occurrences. Let's look at the two absolute foundations of this language: **Sample Space** and **Events**.

---

## 1. The Core Analogy: The "Menu of Outcomes"

Imagine walking into a restaurant. Before you order, you look at the menu. The menu lists every single dish the kitchen can possibly prepare for you. 

This menu is what mathematicians call the **Sample Space**:

> 💡 **Mental Model**: The **Sample Space** (denoted by $S$) is the set of **all possible outcomes** of a random experiment. If you perform the experiment, the outcome *must* be one of the items on this list.

### 🍽️ Examples from the Menu
1. **Flipping a single coin**:
   The menu has only two dishes:
   $$S = \{H, T\}$$
   where $H$ is heads and $T$ is tails.
2. **Rolling a single six-sided die**:
   The menu has six choices:
   $$S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$$
3. **Flipping two coins**:
   The menu has four combinations:
   $$S = \{(H, H), (H, T), (T, H), (T, T)\}$$
4. **Rolling two dice**:
   The menu has 36 possible ordered pairs, starting from $(1,1)$ all the way to $(6,6)$:
   $$S = \{(i, j) \quad \text{where } i, j \in \{1, \dots, 6\}\}$$
5. **Measuring the lifetime of a car (Continuous)**:
   The menu has an infinite number of choices because time is continuous:
   $$S = [0, \infty)$$
   *(Note: The interval $[0, \infty)$ represents any real number greater than or equal to 0).*

---

## 2. Events: Choosing a "Sub-Category"

Now, suppose you are a vegetarian. You look at the menu ($S$) and select only the dishes that don't contain meat. This sub-list is an **Event**.

> 💡 **Mental Model**: An **Event** (denoted by $E$) is any **subset** of the sample space $S$. 
> We say that the event $E$ **occurs** if the actual outcome of the experiment is one of the outcomes in your selected subset.

### 🍽️ Examples of Sub-lists (Events)
* In coin flipping: $E = \{H\}$ is the event that a head appears.
* In rolling a single die: $E = \{2, 4, 6\}$ is the event that an even number appears.
* In rolling two dice: $E = \{(1, 6), (2, 5), (3, 4), (4, 3), (5, 2), (6, 1)\}$ is the event that the sum of the dice equals 7.
* In measuring car lifetime: $E = (2, 6)$ is the event that the car lasts between 2 and 6 years.

---

## 3. Set Operations (Building new events)

Just like combining different criteria to find a dish (e.g., "cheap" and "vegetarian"), we can combine events using set operations:

### A. Union ($E \cup F$) - The "Either/Or" Buffet
The union of event $E$ and event $F$, written as $E \cup F$, consists of all outcomes that are in $E$, in $F$, or in both.
* **Occurrence**: $E \cup F$ occurs if **either** $E$ occurs **or** $F$ occurs (or both).
* **Example**: On a die roll, let $E = \{1, 3, 5\}$ (odd numbers) and $F = \{1, 2, 3\}$ (numbers 3 or less). 
  $$E \cup F = \{1, 2, 3, 5\}$$

### B. Intersection ($EF$ or $E \cap F$) - The "Both" Filter
The intersection of event $E$ and event $F$, written as $EF$ or $E \cap F$, consists of all outcomes that are in **both** $E$ and $F$.
* **Occurrence**: $EF$ occurs only if **both** $E$ and $F$ occur.
* **Example**: For $E = \{1, 3, 5\}$ and $F = \{1, 2, 3\}$:
  $$EF = \{1, 3\}$$

### C. Complement ($E^c$) - The "Not" Filter
The complement of event $E$, written as $E^c$, consists of all outcomes in the sample space $S$ that are **not** in $E$.
* **Occurrence**: $E^c$ occurs if and only if $E$ does not occur.
* **Example**: If $E$ is the event that two dice sum to 7, then $E^c$ is the event that the sum does not equal 7.

### D. Mutually Exclusive ($EF = \varnothing$) - Disjoint Categories
If two events $E$ and $F$ have no outcomes in common, their intersection is the **null event** ($\varnothing$, an empty set with zero outcomes). 
* **Meaning**: They cannot both happen at the same time. They are **mutually exclusive** or **disjoint**.
* **Example**: Flipping a single coin. The events $E = \{H\}$ and $F = \{T\}$ are mutually exclusive because a coin cannot land both heads and tails on a single flip ($EF = \varnothing$).

---

## 4. Generalization to Infinite Sequences

Sometimes, we need to track infinite processes, like tossing a coin forever until a head appears.

### Infinite Union ($\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty E_n$)
The event that consists of all outcomes that are in $E_n$ for **at least one** value of $n = 1, 2, \dots$.
$$\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty E_n$$

### Infinite Intersection ($\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty E_n$)
The event consisting of those outcomes that are in **all** of the events $E_n$, for $n = 1, 2, \dots$.
$$\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty E_n$$
