# 03. Conditional Probability (Section 1.4)

What happens to probability when we get new information? 
If you are guessing the weather, the probability of rain is different if you see clouds in the sky. This updated probability is what we call **Conditional Probability**.

---

## 1. The Core Analogy: E-Commerce Filters

Imagine you are shopping online for a pair of shoes. The website has 10,000 items in total (this is your original Sample Space $S$).
1. You apply a filter: **"Only show Shoes."**
2. Immediately, the active universe of items shrinks to 500 shoe items (this is event $F$, your new sample space).
3. Now, you ask: **"What is the probability of picking a RED item?"**
4. You don't calculate this relative to the original 10,000 items. You calculate it only out of the 500 shoes.
5. The only red items left are the ones that are **both** red **and** shoes (the intersection $EF$).

This is the exact logic of the conditional probability formula:

> **Definition**: The conditional probability of $E$ occurring, given that $F$ has occurred, is written as $P(E|F)$ and is defined as:
> $$P(E|F) = \frac{P(EF)}{P(F)}$$
> where $P(F) > 0$.

```
Original Space (S):  [   10,000 Items total   ]
Filtered Space (F):  [      500 Shoes         ]
Intersection (EF):   [      50 Red Shoes      ]
Conditional Prob:    50 / 500 = 10%
```

---

## 2. The Multiplication Rule

If we multiply both sides of the conditional probability formula by $P(F)$, we get the **Multiplication Rule**:
$$P(EF) = P(F) P(E|F)$$
* **Intuition**: For both $E$ and $F$ to happen, first $F$ must happen (with probability $P(F)$), and then, knowing that $F$ has happened, $E$ must happen (with probability $P(E|F)$).

---

## 3. Walkthrough Examples

Let's look at classic problems where this logic shows up.

### Example A: The Two Children Paradox (Example 1.5)
* **Problem**: A family has two children. What is the conditional probability that both are boys given that at least one of them is a boy?
* **First-Time Trap**: You might think, "Well, the other child can be a boy or a girl, so the probability is $\frac{1}{2}$." Let's walk through the math to see why this is incorrect.
* **Textbook Walkthrough**:
  1. The original sample space of gender arrangements (ordered from older to younger) is:
     $$S = \{(b, b), (b, g), (g, b), (g, g)\}$$
     Since each arrangement is equally likely, each has a probability of $\frac{1}{4}$.
  2. We are given the condition: **at least one of them is a boy**. Let's call this event $A$:
     $$A = \{(b, b), (b, g), (g, b)\}$$
     This is our filtered universe. Its probability is $P(A) = \frac{3}{4}$.
  3. We want to find the probability that **both are boys** ($B = \{(b, b)\}$), given this condition.
  4. The outcomes that satisfy both being boys *and* at least one being a boy is just $BA = \{(b, b)\}$, with probability $P(BA) = \frac{1}{4}$.
  5. Applying the formula:
     $$P(B|A) = \frac{P(BA)}{P(A)} = \frac{1/4}{3/4} = \frac{1}{3}$$
* **Conclusion**: Because the condition "at least one is a boy" eliminates the $(g, g)$ outcome, we are left with 3 equally likely options. Only 1 of those 3 options is $(b, b)$, making the probability $\frac{1}{3}$.

### Example B: Urn Draws without Replacement (Example 1.7)
* **Problem**: An urn contains 7 black balls and 5 white balls. We draw two balls without replacement. What is the probability that both are black?
* **Walkthrough**:
  - Let $F$ be the event that the first ball drawn is black.
  - Let $E$ be the event that the second ball drawn is black.
  - The probability that the first ball is black is simple:
    $$P(F) = \frac{7 \text{ black balls}}{12 \text{ total balls}} = \frac{7}{12}$$
  - Since we do not replace the ball, if the first was black, the urn now contains 6 black balls and 5 white balls (11 total). The conditional probability of drawing a second black ball is:
    $$P(E|F) = \frac{6}{11}$$
  - Using the multiplication rule, the probability of both being black ($P(EF)$) is:
    $$P(EF) = P(F) P(E|F) = \frac{7}{12} \cdot \frac{6}{11} = \frac{42}{132} = \frac{7}{22}$$

### Example C: The Matching Hats Problem (Example 1.8)
* **Problem**: Three men throw their hats into the center of a room, mix them up, and randomly select one each. What is the probability that **none** of the three men selects his own hat?
* **Walkthrough**:
  1. We solve this by finding the probability of the opposite event: **at least one man gets his own hat**.
  2. Let $E_i$ be the event that the $i$-th man selects his own hat ($i=1, 2, 3$).
  3. For any single man, the probability is: $P(E_i) = \frac{1}{3}$ (since there are 3 hats and 1 is his).
  4. For any two specific men both getting their own hats:
     - The first gets his with probability $P(E_i) = \frac{1}{3}$.
     - If the first got his, there are 2 hats left, so the second gets his with conditional probability $P(E_j | E_i) = \frac{1}{2}$.
     - Thus: $P(E_i E_j) = P(E_i) P(E_j | E_i) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{6}$.
  5. For all three men getting their own hats:
     - First gets his ($\frac{1}{3}$), second gets his ($\frac{1}{2}$).
     - Once the first two have their own hats, the third man's hat is the only one remaining. He gets it with probability $1$.
     - Thus: $P(E_1 E_2 E_3) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot 1 = \frac{1}{6}$.
  6. Apply the Inclusion-Exclusion formula for 3 events:
     $$P(E_1 \cup E_2 \cup E_3) = \left[P(E_1) + P(E_2) + P(E_3)\right] - \left[P(E_1 E_2) + P(E_1 E_3) + P(E_2 E_3)\right] + P(E_1 E_2 E_3)$$
     $$P(E_1 \cup E_2 \cup E_3) = \left[\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3}\right] - \left[\frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6}\right] + \frac{1}{6} = 1 - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{2}{3}$$
  7. The probability that **no one** gets their own hat is the complement:
     $$P(\text{none}) = 1 - P(E_1 \cup E_2 \cup E_3) = 1 - \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$$
