# 06. Basic Probability Practice Problems (Exercises)

This guide provides step-by-step walkthroughs and conceptual solutions for the exercises at the end of Chapter 1 of the textbook.

---

## 📝 Problem Set & Step-by-Step Solutions

### Exercise 1: Drawing Marbles With Replacement
* **Problem**: A box contains three marbles: one red ($R$), one green ($G$), and one blue ($B$). We take one marble, replace it, and draw a second. What is the sample space? If all marbles are equally likely, what is the probability of each point in the sample space?
* **Walkthrough**:
  1. Since we **replace** the marble, the pool of choices for both draws remains identical: $\{R, G, B\}$.
  2. The sample space $S$ consists of ordered pairs $(first\_draw, second\_draw)$:
     $$S = \{(R, R), (R, G), (R, B), (G, R), (G, G), (G, B), (B, R), (B, G), (B, B)\}$$
  3. The total number of outcomes is $|S| = 3 \times 3 = 9$.
  4. Because the trials are independent and outcomes are equally likely, each point has probability:
     $$P(\text{each outcome}) = \frac{1}{9}$$

### Exercise 2: Drawing Marbles Without Replacement
* **Problem**: Repeat Exercise 1 when the second marble is drawn without replacement.
* **Walkthrough**:
  1. Since we **do not replace** the marble, we cannot draw the same marble twice. Outcomes like $(R, R)$, $(G, G)$, and $(B, B)$ are removed.
  2. The sample space is:
     $$S = \{(R, G), (R, B), (G, R), (G, B), (B, R), (B, G)\}$$
  3. The total number of outcomes is $|S| = 3 \times 2 = 6$.
  4. Each point in the sample space has probability:
     $$P(\text{each outcome}) = \frac{1}{6}$$

### Exercise 3: Toss Until Heads Twice in a Row
* **Problem**: A coin is tossed until a head appears twice in a row. What is the sample space? If the coin is fair, what is the probability that it will be tossed exactly four times?
* **Walkthrough**:
  1. **Building the Sample Space ($S$)**: Let $H$ be heads and $T$ be tails. The game ends as soon as $HH$ appears.
     - 2 tosses: $HH$
     - 3 tosses: $THH$ (cannot be $HHH$ because it would have stopped on the first two tosses)
     - 4 tosses: $TTHH, HTHH$ (cannot be $HHTH$ as it would have stopped at flip 2)
     - The general sample space:
       $$S = \{HH, THH, TTHH, HTHH, TTTHH, HTTTHH, THTTHH, \dots\}$$
  2. **Exactly 4 tosses probability**:
     - The two target outcomes are $A = \{TTHH, HTHH\}$.
     - Since the coin is fair, each toss is independent with probability $P(H) = P(T) = \frac{1}{2}$.
     - The probability of any specific sequence of length 4 is $\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^4 = \frac{1}{16}$.
     - Adding these mutually exclusive paths gives:
       $$P(\text{exactly 4 tosses}) = P(\{TTHH\}) + P(\{HTHH\}) = \frac{1}{16} + \frac{1}{16} = \frac{2}{16} = \frac{1}{8}$$

### Exercise 4: Set Notation for 3 Events ($E, F, G$)
* **Problem**: Express the following events in set notation:
  - **(a) only $F$ occurs**:
    - *Logic*: $F$ must occur, AND $E$ must not occur ($E^c$), AND $G$ must not occur ($G^c$).
    - *Notation*: $F E^c G^c$ (or $F \cap E^c \cap G^c$)
  - **(b) both $E$ and $F$ but not $G$ occur**:
    - *Notation*: $EF G^c$
  - **(c) at least one event occurs**:
    - *Logic*: The union of all three.
    - *Notation*: $E \cup F \cup G$
  - **(d) at least two events occur**:
    - *Logic*: At least one pair of events occurs.
    - *Notation*: $EF \cup EG \cup FG$
  - **(e) all three events occur**:
    - *Notation*: $EFG$
  - **(f) none occurs**:
    - *Notation*: $(E \cup F \cup G)^c$ or $E^c F^c G^c$
  - **(g) at most one occurs**:
    - *Logic*: Either zero occur, or exactly one occurs. This is the complement of "at least two occur":
    - *Notation*: $(EF \cup EG \cup FG)^c$
  - **(h) at most two occur**:
    - *Logic*: The complement of "all three occur":
    - *Notation*: $(EFG)^c$ or $E^c \cup F^c \cup G^c$

### Exercise 5: Roulette Gambling System
* **Problem**: A player bets $1 on red in roulette. If he wins, he quits. If he loses, he bets $2 a second time, and then regardless of outcome, quits. Assuming the probability of winning each bet is $\frac{1}{2}$, what is the probability he goes home a winner? Why is this system not used by everyone?
* **Walkthrough**:
  1. Let $W_1$ be winning the first bet ($P(W_1) = 1/2$), and $L_1$ be losing the first bet ($P(L_1) = 1/2$).
  2. If he wins the first bet, he gains $+1$ and quits.
  3. If he loses the first bet (net $-1$), he plays a second time. Let $W_2$ be winning the second bet ($P(W_2|L_1) = 1/2$).
     - If he wins, he gains $+2$. His net profit is: $-1 + 2 = +1$.
     - If he loses, he loses $-2$. His net profit is: $-1 - 2 = -3$.
  4. The event that he goes home a winner is $W = W_1 \cup (L_1 W_2)$.
  5. Calculate the probability:
     $$P(W) = P(W_1) + P(L_1)P(W_2 | L_1) = \frac{1}{2} + \left(\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4} = 75\%$$
  6. **Why it's not used by everyone**:
     - Calculate the Expected Value (average outcome) of the game:
       $$E(\text{Profit}) = (+1) \cdot P(W) + (-3) \cdot P(L_1 L_2) = 1 \cdot \left(\frac{3}{4}\right) + (-3) \cdot \left(\frac{1}{4}\right) = \frac{3}{4} - \frac{3}{4} = 0$$
     - The game is completely fair (average profit is 0). While you win 75% of the time, your wins are very small ($+1$). Your losses (25% chance) are 3 times larger ($-3$).
     - In real casinos, roulette wheels have a green $0$ or $00$, making the probability of winning less than $\frac{1}{2}$ ($18/38 \approx 47.4\%$). This pushes the expected profit below 0, guaranteeing that the player will lose money on average.

### Exercise 6: Distributive Law Proof
* **Problem**: Show that $E(F \cup G) = EF \cup EG$.
* **Walkthrough (Proof by Double Containment)**:
  - **Part A ($E(F \cup G) \subseteq EF \cup EG$)**:
    - Let element $x$ be in the set $E(F \cup G)$.
    - This means $x \in E$ AND $x \in (F \cup G)$.
    - Since $x \in (F \cup G)$, $x$ must be in $F$ or in $G$.
    - If $x \in F$, then since $x \in E$, $x \in EF$.
    - If $x \in G$, then since $x \in E$, $x \in EG$.
    - Therefore, $x$ must be in the union $EF \cup EG$.
  - **Part B ($EF \cup EG \subseteq E(F \cup G)$)**:
    - Let element $y$ be in the set $EF \cup EG$.
    - This means $y$ is in $EF$ OR $y$ is in $EG$.
    - If $y \in EF$, then $y \in E$ and $y \in F$. Since $y \in F$, $y \in (F \cup G)$. Thus, $y \in E(F \cup G)$.
    - If $y \in EG$, then $y \in E$ and $y \in G$. Since $y \in G$, $y \in (F \cup G)$. Thus, $y \in E(F \cup G)$.
    - Therefore, $y$ must be in the set $E(F \cup G)$.
  - Since both container checks hold, $E(F \cup G) = EF \cup EG \quad \blacksquare$

### Exercise 7: De Morgan's Law Proof
* **Problem**: Show that $(E \cup F)^c = E^c F^c$.
* **Walkthrough (Proof by Double Containment)**:
  - **Part A ($(E \cup F)^c \subseteq E^c F^c$)**:
    - Let $x \in (E \cup F)^c$.
    - This means $x$ is not in the union $E \cup F$.
    - Since $x$ is not in the union, $x$ cannot be in $E$ AND $x$ cannot be in $F$.
    - This means $x \in E^c$ and $x \in F^c$.
    - Thus, $x \in E^c F^c$.
  - **Part B ($E^c F^c \subseteq (E \cup F)^c$)**:
    - Let $y \in E^c F^c$.
    - This means $y \in E^c$ and $y \in F^c$.
    - Therefore, $y$ is not in $E$ AND $y$ is not in $F$.
    - Since it is in neither, $y$ cannot be in their union: $y \notin E \cup F$.
    - Thus, $y \in (E \cup F)^c$.
  - Since both container checks hold, $(E \cup F)^c = E^c F^c \quad \blacksquare$
