# 03. Continuous Random Variables (Section 2.3)

In the discrete world, we assign weights to individual points. But what happens when we measure things that are continuousвҖ”like the exact height of a tree, the lifetime of a computer, or the temperature in a room? 

This transition leads us to **continuous random variables**, which bring a fascinating mathematical paradox.

---

## 1. The Core Paradox: The "Exact Height" Analogy

Suppose you ask: **What is the probability that a person is exactly 175 cm tall?**
At first, you might say it's quite common. But let's look closer. 
* Is the person *exactly* 175.00000000... cm tall? 
* What about 175.000001 cm? Or 174.9999999 cm?
* As we measure with infinite precision, the number of possible exact heights becomes infinite. 

> рҹ’Ў **The Paradox**: The probability of a continuous random variable taking on any **single, exact value is zero**:
> $$P(X = 175) = 0$$

If every single exact value has a probability of 0, how does anything happen? 
We resolve this by shifting our focus from **points** to **intervals**. We can never measure *exactly* 175 cm, but we can measure someone between $174.5$ cm and $175.5$ cm. 

---

## 2. Probability Density Function (PDF) vs. Probability

To describe continuous variables, we use a **Probability Density Function (PDF)**, written as $f(x)$.

> **Analogy: Terrain Density vs. Actual Weight**
> Think of the PDF $f(x)$ as a map showing the **density of soil** on a plot of land. 
> - The height of the curve at a point $x$ (soil density) is **not** a probability. It can even be greater than 1!
> - The **probability** is represented by the **actual weight of the soil** over an interval $[a, b]$, which is the **area under the curve** (the integral):
>   $$P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx$$

```

```

### Properties of the PDF $f(x)$:
1. The density can never be negative: $f(x) \ge 0$.
2. The total area under the entire curve must equal exactly 1 (100% chance that $X$ takes *some* value):
   $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) \, dx = 1$$

### Relation between CDF and PDF
The Cumulative Distribution Function $F(a)$ accumulates the area from the far left up to $a$:
$$F(a) = P(X \le a) = \int_{-\infty}^a f(x) \, dx$$
By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, the derivative of the progress bar (CDF) is the density (PDF):
$$\frac{d}{da} F(a) = f(a)$$

## 3. The Three Key Continuous Distributions

### A. The Uniform Distribution (Equally Likely Anywhere)
* **The Scenario**: The variable is bounded between $\alpha$ and $\beta$, and has the exact same likelihood of landing in any subinterval of the same length.
* **Analogy**: A perfectly balanced spinner. The pointer is equally likely to stop anywhere.
* **PDF**:
  $$f(x) = \begin{cases}
  \frac{1}{\beta - \alpha}, & \text{if } \alpha < x < \beta \\
  0, & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}$$
* **CDF**:
  $$F(a) = \begin{cases}
  0, & a \le \alpha \\
  \frac{a - \alpha}{\beta - \alpha}, & \alpha < a < \beta \\
  1, & a \ge \beta
  \end{cases}$$

### B. The Exponential Distribution (Decay and Waiting Time)
* **The Scenario**: Used to model the time we must wait for a completely random event to occur (like the failure of a machine, the arrival of a customer, or radioactive decay).
* **Analogy**: The lifetime of a light bulb. It is highly likely to fail early, and the likelihood of surviving decreases exponentially over time.
* **PDF** (for $x \ge 0$):
  $$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$$
* **CDF** (probability of failing before time $a$):
  $$F(a) = 1 - e^{-\lambda a}$$

*(Note: Gamma distribution section 2.3.3 is omitted per syllabus guidelines).*

### C. The Normal Distribution (The Bell Curve)
* **The Scenario**: The classic symmetric, bell-shaped distribution. It occurs whenever an outcome is the sum of many small, independent random forces (like test scores, physical heights, or measurement errors).
* **PDF**:
  $$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}, \quad -\infty < x < \infty$$
  where $\mu$ is the center (mean) and $\sigma^2$ controls the width (variance).

---

## 4. Normal Linear Transformation Proof

> рҹ’Ў **Why this matters**: In statistics, we standardise scores. For example, if a test has mean $\mu = 70$ and standard deviation $\sigma = 10$, we subtract 70 and divide by 10 to get a "Z-score". We do this because of a vital theorem: **shifting and scaling a normal variable keeps it normal**.

Let's prove this step-by-step:
Let $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, and we define $Y = \alpha X + \beta$ (assuming $\alpha > 0$). We want to find the distribution of $Y$.

1. **Write the CDF of $Y$**:
   $$F_Y(a) = P(Y \le a) = P(\alpha X + \beta \le a)$$
2. **Isolate $X$**:
   $$F_Y(a) = P\left(X \le \frac{a - \beta}{\alpha}\right) = F_X\left(\frac{a - \beta}{\alpha}\right)$$
3. **Write the integral for the normal CDF of $X$**:
   $$F_Y(a) = \int_{-\infty}^{(a-\beta)/\alpha} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \, dx$$
4. **Change Variables**: Let's substitute $v = \alpha x + \beta$. This means $x = \frac{v-\beta}{\alpha}$, and differentiating gives $dx = \frac{dv}{\alpha}$.
   - When $x = -\infty$, $v = -\infty$.
   - When $x = \frac{a-\beta}{\alpha}$, $v = a$.
5. **Substitute back into the integral**:
   $$F_Y(a) = \int_{-\infty}^{a} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma} e^{-\frac{(\frac{v-\beta}{\alpha}-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \, \frac{dv}{\alpha}$$
   Pull the constant $\alpha$ into the denominator:
   $$F_Y(a) = \int_{-\infty}^{a} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}(\alpha\sigma)} e^{-\frac{(v - (\alpha\mu + \beta))^2}{2(\alpha\sigma)^2}} \, dv$$
6. Since the CDF of $Y$ is the integral of its density $f_Y(v)$, we differentiate to get the PDF:
   $$f_Y(v) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}(\alpha\sigma)} e^{-\frac{(v - (\alpha\mu + \beta))^2}{2(\alpha\sigma)^2}}$$
7. **Conclusion**: This is exactly the formula of a normal distribution with mean $\alpha\mu + \beta$ and variance $(\alpha\sigma)^2 = \alpha^2\sigma^2$. Shifting and scaling preserves normality!

This proves that if you subtract the mean and divide by the standard deviation:
$$Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}$$
the result $Z$ is a **Standard Normal Variable** ($Z \sim N(0, 1)$), which has mean 0 and variance 1.
