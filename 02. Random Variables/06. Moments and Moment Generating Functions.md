# 06. Moments and Moment Generating Functions

In statistics, we often want to describe the shape of a probability distribution without writing out its entire PDF or PMF. We do this using **Moments**. To make calculating these moments easier, we use a powerful mathematical tool called the **Moment Generating Function (MGF)**.

---

## 1. What are Moments?

Moments describe the shape, center, and spread of a distribution.

### A. Raw Moments (About the Origin)
The $k$-th raw moment of a random variable $X$ is the expected value of $X^k$:
$$\mu'_k = \text{E}[X^k]$$
* **1st Raw Moment ($k=1$):** $\mu'_1 = \text{E}[X]$, which is the **Mean** (center of mass).

### B. Central Moments (About the Mean)
The $k$-th central moment of $X$ is the expected value of the deviation from the mean $\mu = \text{E}[X]$ raised to the $k$-th power:
$$\mu_k = \text{E}[(X - \mu)^k]$$
* **1st Central Moment ($k=1$):** $\text{E}[X - \mu] = \text{E}[X] - \mu = 0$ (always zero).
* **2nd Central Moment ($k=2$):** $\mu_2 = \text{E}[(X - \mu)^2]$, which is the **Variance** (spread).

### Physical Interpretation of Moments:
1. **1st Moment:** Mean (where the center of mass lies).
2. **2nd Moment:** Variance (how spread out the mass is from the center).
3. **3rd Moment (Skewness):** Measure of asymmetry (leaning to the left or right).
4. **4th Moment (Kurtosis):** Measure of "tailedness" (how heavy or thick the tails of the distribution are).

---

## 2. Moment Generating Functions (MGFs)

The **Moment Generating Function (MGF)** of a random variable $X$, denoted by $M_X(t)$, is defined as:
$$M_X(t) = \text{E}[e^{tX}], \quad \text{for } t \in \mathbb{R}$$

* **Discrete Case:** $M_X(t) = \sum_{x} e^{tx} p(x)$
* **Continuous Case:** $M_X(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{tx} f(x) \, dx$

---

## 3. Why is it called a "Generating" Function?

By writing the Taylor series expansion of $e^{tX}$ around $0$:
$$e^{tX} = 1 + tX + \frac{(tX)^2}{2!} + \frac{(tX)^3}{3!} + \dots + \frac{(tX)^k}{k!} + \dots$$

If we take the expectation of both sides (assuming we can exchange the expectation and the sum):
$$\text{E}[e^{tX}] = \text{E}[1] + t\text{E}[X] + \frac{t^2}{2!}\text{E}[X^2] + \frac{t^3}{3!}\text{E}[X^3] + \dots + \frac{t^k}{k!}\text{E}[X^k] + \dots$$

$$M_X(t) = 1 + t\mu'_1 + \frac{t^2}{2!}\mu'_2 + \frac{t^3}{3!}\mu'_3 + \dots + \frac{t^k}{k!}\mu'_k + \dots$$

Notice that the **coefficient of $\frac{t^k}{k!}$** in the Taylor series of $M_X(t)$ is exactly the $k$-th raw moment $\text{E}[X^k]$!

### Extracting Moments using Derivatives:
To isolate the $k$-th raw moment, we differentiate $M_X(t)$ exactly $k$ times with respect to $t$, and then evaluate the derivative at $t = 0$:
$$\text{E}[X^k] = \left. \frac{d^k}{dt^k} M_X(t) \right|_{t=0}$$

#### First Moment (Mean):
$$\text{E}[X] = M'_X(0)$$

#### Second Moment:
$$\text{E}[X^2] = M''_X(0)$$

#### Variance Formula:
$$\text{Var}(X) = \text{E}[X^2] - (\text{E}[X])^2 = M''_X(0) - [M'_X(0)]^2$$

---

## 4. Crucial Properties of MGFs

MGFs are incredibly useful due to three core properties:

### A. Uniqueness Theorem
If two random variables $X$ and $Y$ have the same MGF for all $t$ in a neighborhood of 0, they have the **exact same probability distribution**. This is useful for proving that a sum of variables follows a specific distribution.

### B. Linear Transformations
If $Y = aX + b$, then:
$$M_Y(t) = \text{E}[e^{t(aX + b)}] = \text{E}[e^{tbt} e^{atX}] = e^{bt}\text{E}[e^{(at)X}] = e^{bt} M_X(at)$$

### C. Sum of Independent Random Variables
If $X$ and $Y$ are **independent** random variables, and $Z = X + Y$, then:
$$M_Z(t) = \text{E}[e^{t(X + Y)}] = \text{E}[e^{tX} e^{tY}]$$
Since $X$ and $Y$ are independent, $\text{E}[g(X)h(Y)] = \text{E}[g(X)]\text{E}[h(Y)]$:
$$M_Z(t) = \text{E}[e^{tX}]\text{E}[e^{tY}] = M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t)$$
* **Rule:** The MGF of the sum of independent variables is the product of their individual MGFs.

## 5. MGF Table for Core Distributions

| Distribution | MGF $M_X(t)$ | Mean $M'(0)$ | Variance $M''(0) - [M'(0)]^2$ |
| :--- | :--- | :---: | :---: |
| **Bernoulli($p$)** | $1 - p + p e^t$ | $p$ | $p(1-p)$ |
| **Binomial($n, p$)** | $(1 - p + p e^t)^n$ | $np$ | $np(1-p)$ |
| **Poisson($\lambda$)** | $e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$ | $\lambda$ | $\lambda$ |
| **Geometric($p$)** | $\frac{p e^t}{1 - (1-p)e^t}$ | $\frac{1}{p}$ | $\frac{1-p}{p^2}$ |
| **Exponential($\lambda$)** | $\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t} \quad (t < \lambda)$ | $\frac{1}{\lambda}$ | $\frac{1}{\lambda^2}$ |
| **Normal($\mu, \sigma^2$)** | $e^{\mu t + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2}$ | $\mu$ | $\sigma^2$ |
