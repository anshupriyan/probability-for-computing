# 05. Limit Theorems and Inequalities (Section 2.8)

In real life, we rarely know the exact probability distribution of a random system. If you design a network router, you might only know the average number of packets per second, not the exact probability of every packet count. 

This note covers **Inequalities** (which give us safety bounds when we lack detailed information) and **Limit Theorems** (which explain why massive systems behave predictably).

---

## 1. Inequalities: The "Worst-Case Shield"

Imagine you are designing a bridge. You don't know the exact weight distribution of all cars crossing it. However, if you know the **average weight** of a car, can you bound the probability that a car is extremely heavy? Yes!

### A. Markov's Inequality (Using only the Mean)
If a random variable $X$ only takes positive values (non-negative), then the probability that $X$ exceeds some threshold $a$ is bounded by its mean divided by $a$:

> **Markov's Inequality**: For any $a > 0$:
> $$P(X \ge a) \le \frac{E[X]}{a}$$

#### 📝 Step-by-Step Proof (Continuous Case)
Let's see why this holds. Start with the expected value integral:
$$E[X] = \int_{0}^\infty x f(x) \, dx$$
1. **Split the domain**: We divide the integral into two parts, from $0$ to $a$ and from $a$ to $\infty$:
   $$E[X] = \int_{0}^a x f(x) \, dx + \int_{a}^\infty x f(x) \, dx$$
2. **Drop the first part**: Since the variable $X \ge 0$, the first integral $\int_{0}^a x f(x) \, dx$ must be positive or zero. If we discard it, the expression can only decrease:
   $$E[X] \ge \int_{a}^\infty x f(x) \, dx$$
3. **Minimize $x$**: Within the range of integration $[a, \infty)$, the variable $x$ is at least $a$ ($x \ge a$). If we replace $x$ with $a$, the integral can only decrease:
   $$E[X] \ge \int_{a}^\infty a f(x) \, dx$$
4. **Pull out the constant**: Pull the constant $a$ out of the integral:
   $$E[X] \ge a \int_{a}^\infty f(x) \, dx$$
5. **Substitute probability**: Notice that $\int_a^\infty f(x) \, dx$ is by definition the probability $P(X \ge a)$:
   $$E[X] \ge a P(X \ge a) \implies P(X \ge a) \le \frac{E[X]}{a} \quad \blacksquare$$

### B. Chebyshev's Inequality (Using Mean and Variance)
If you know both the mean ($\mu$) and the variance ($\sigma^2$) of a distribution, you can set a much tighter bound on how far $X$ deviates from the mean:

> **Chebyshev's Inequality**: For any $k > 0$:
> $$P(|X - \mu| \ge k) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}$$

#### 📝 Step-by-Step Proof
1. Consider the squared deviation $(X - \mu)^2$. Since this term is squared, it is always non-negative.
2. We can apply Markov's inequality to this squared variable using a threshold of $k^2$:
   $$P\left((X - \mu)^2 \ge k^2\right) \le \frac{E\left[(X - \mu)^2\right]}{k^2}$$
3. The event $(X-\mu)^2 \ge k^2$ is algebraically identical to saying the distance $|X-\mu| \ge k$.
4. The numerator $E[(X-\mu)^2]$ is the definition of the variance $\sigma^2$.
5. Substituting these in gives:
   $$P(|X - \mu| \ge k) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2} \quad \blacksquare$$

## 2. Limit Theorems: The "Bell-Curve Machine"

### A. The Strong Law of Large Numbers (SLLN)
* **The Concept**: If you perform an independent experiment over and over again, the running average of your outcomes will eventually converge to the true theoretical mean.
* **Analogy**: The "Truth-Will-Out" principle. If you flip a fair coin 10 times, you might get 80% heads (deviation). If you flip it 10 million times, the proportion of heads will be almost exactly 50%.
* **Formula**:
  $$\frac{X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_n}{n} \to \mu \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

### B. The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)
* **The Concept**: Take any group of random variables. If you sum them up, the sum will always look like a normal distribution (bell curve) as the count grows. There are two primary formulations you will encounter:

#### 1. Lindeberg-Lévy Central Limit Theorem (i.i.d. Case)
This is the standard, modern version of the CLT. It applies to any sequence of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables.
> **Lindeberg-Lévy CLT Statement**: Let $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$ be a sequence of i.i.d. random variables with finite expectation $\text{E}[X_i] = \mu$ and finite variance $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 > 0$. As $n \to \infty$, the distribution of the standardized sample mean (or sum) approaches the standard normal distribution $N(0, 1)$:
> $$Z_n = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n X_i - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$$
> Equivalently, for any real number $a$:
> $$\lim_{n \to \infty} P(Z_n \le a) = \Phi(a)$$

#### 2. De Moivre–Laplace Central Limit Theorem (Binomial Case)
This is a historic precursor to the general CLT. It is a special case that applies specifically to Binomial random variables (sums of Bernoulli trials).
> **De Moivre–Laplace CLT Statement**: Let $X_n$ be a Binomial random variable with parameters $n$ and $p$ (i.e., $X_n = \sum_{i=1}^n B_i$ where $B_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ are independent). As $n \to \infty$, the standardized binomial variable converges in distribution to the standard normal distribution $N(0, 1)$:
> $$Z_n = \frac{X_n - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$$
> This justifies the **Normal Approximation to the Binomial** with continuity correction: $P(X_n = k) \approx P(k - 0.5 \le Y \le k + 0.5)$ where $Y \sim N(np, np(1-p))$.

#### 📝 Heuristic Proof of Lindeberg-Lévy CLT using Moment Generating Functions (MGFs)
Let's assume $\mu=0$ and $\sigma^2=1$. Let $M(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ be the MGF of each $X_i$. 
We want to find the MGF of the normalized sum $Y_n = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n X_i}{\sqrt{n}}$:

1. **Calculate the MGF of $Y_n$**:
   $$M_{Y_n}(t) = E\left[e^{t \frac{\sum X_i}{\sqrt{n}}}\right] = E\left[\prod_{i=1}^n e^{\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}} X_i}\right] = \left(M\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right)^n \quad \text{(due to independence)}$$
2. **Expand $M(y)$ using Taylor Series around 0**:
   $$M(y) = E[e^{yX}] = E\left[1 + yX + \frac{y^2 X^2}{2} + o(y^2)\right] = 1 + yE[X] + \frac{y^2}{2}E[X^2] + o(y^2)$$
   Since $E[X] = 0$ (mean) and $E[X^2] = 1$ (variance + mean$^2$):
   $$M(y) = 1 + \frac{y^2}{2} + o(y^2)$$
3. **Substitute $y = \frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}$**:
   $$M\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = 1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + o\left(\frac{t^2}{n}\right)$$
4. **Take the limit as $n \to \infty$**:
   $$\lim_{n \to \infty} M_{Y_n}(t) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + o\left(\frac{t^2}{n}\right)\right)^n$$
   Recall the calculus limit identity $\lim_{n \to \infty} (1 + x/n)^n = e^x$. This yields:
   $$\lim_{n \to \infty} M_{Y_n}(t) = e^{t^2/2}$$
5. **Conclusion**: Since $e^{t^2/2}$ is the unique MGF of the standard normal distribution $N(0, 1)$, the normalized sum must converge to standard normal $\Phi \quad \blacksquare$

## 3. Real-World Walkthrough: Stockpile Lifetime (Example 2.52)

* **Problem**: A factory uses batteries one by one. The lifetime of a battery has a mean of 40 hours and a standard deviation of 20 hours. If they have a stockpile of 25 batteries, what is the probability that the total operation time exceeds 1100 hours?
* **Concept Breakdown**:
  1. Let $X_i$ be the lifetime of battery $i$. We want to find $P(\sum_{i=1}^{25} X_i > 1100)$.
  2. The sum of 25 independent batteries $S_{25} = \sum X_i$ can be approximated by a normal distribution because of the CLT.
  3. **Find the parameters of the sum**:
     - Expected total lifetime: $E[S_{25}] = n\mu = 25 \times 40 = 1000$ hours.
     - Total variance: $\text{Var}(S_{25}) = n\sigma^2 = 25 \times 20^2 = 10,000$.
     - Total standard deviation: $\text{SD}(S_{25}) = \sqrt{10,000} = 100$ hours.
  4. **Standardize the target**:
     $$P(S_{25} > 1100) = P\left(\frac{S_{25} - 1000}{100} > \frac{1100 - 1000}{100}\right) \approx P(Z > 1)$$
  5. **Evaluate using standard normal tables**:
     $$P(Z > 1) = 1 - \Phi(1) \approx 1 - 0.8413 = 0.1587$$
  6. **Conclusion**: There is approximately a 15.87% chance that your stockpile of 25 batteries will last over 1100 hours.
