### Question 1

**(a) Let E, F, G be three events. Find expression for the events that of E, F, G:**
**(i) Both E and F but not G occur**
**Solution:** $E \cap F \cap G'$ (where $G'$ is the complement of $G$).
**(ii) At least one events occur**
**Solution:** $E \cup F \cup G$
**(iii) At most two occur**
**Solution:** This is the complement of all three occurring: $(E \cap F \cap G)'$ or $E' \cup F' \cup G'$.
**(iv) All three events occur**
**Solution:** $E \cap F \cap G$
**Concepts:** [[01. Sample Space and Events]]

**(b) (i) When are two states said to communicate with each other?**
**Solution:**
Two states $i$ and $j$ in a Markov chain communicate if there is a positive probability of reaching state $j$ from state $i$ in a finite number of steps, and also a positive probability of reaching state $i$ from state $j$. This is denoted as $i \leftrightarrow j$.
**(ii) For the given transition probability matrix of a four-state Markov chain with states 0,1,2, and 3, answer the following:**
$$P = \begin{bmatrix} 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 & 0 \\ 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 & 0 \\ 1/4 & 1/4 & 1/4 & 1/4 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$
**(a) Which state is an absorbing state?**
**Solution:** State 3 is absorbing because $P_{33} = 1$, meaning once the process enters state 3, it never leaves.
**(b) Do states 0 and 2 communicate?**
**Solution:** From state 2, we can reach state 0 ($P_{20} = 1/4 > 0$). But from state 0, there is no path to state 2. Since state 2 is not accessible from state 0, they do not communicate.
**(c) Do states 0 and 1 communicate?**
**Solution:** Yes. $P_{01} = 1/2 > 0$ and $P_{10} = 1/2 > 0$. They can reach each other.
**Concepts:** [[03. Classification of States]]

**(c) What is Random number? Write the name of one approach to define the Random number.**
**Solution:**
A random number is a number chosen from a pool of limited or unlimited numbers that has no discernible pattern for prediction. In computing, we typically use pseudo-random numbers, which are generated deterministically but appear random.
One approach is the **Linear Congruential Generator (LCG)**.
**Concepts:** [[05. Random Number Generation and Simulation]]

**(d) There are n components. On a rainy day, component i will function with probability $p_i$; on a non-rainy day, component i will function with probability $q_i$, for $i = 1, 2, \ldots, n$. It will rain tomorrow with probability $\alpha$. Calculate conditional expected number of components that function tomorrow, given that it rains.**
**Solution:**
Let $X_i$ be an indicator variable equal to 1 if component $i$ functions tomorrow, and 0 otherwise.
We are given that it rains tomorrow. Therefore, the probability that component $i$ functions is $P(X_i=1 | \text{Rain}) = p_i$.
The expected number of functioning components given rain is $E\left[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \mid \text{Rain}\right] = \sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i | \text{Rain}]$.
Since $E[X_i | \text{Rain}] = 1 \cdot p_i + 0 \cdot (1-p_i) = p_i$,
The conditional expected number is $\sum_{i=1}^n p_i$.
**Concepts:** [[04. Expectation and Variance]]

**(e) Write down the probability mass function of the following distributions:**
**(i) Binomial Random variable**
**Solution:** For a Binomial random variable with parameters $n$ and $p$:
$P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n$.
**(ii) Geometric Random Variable**
**Solution:** For a Geometric random variable representing the number of trials until the first success (with success probability $p$):
$P(X=k) = (1-p)^{k-1} p$ for $k = 1, 2, \ldots$
**Concepts:** [[02. Discrete Random Variables]]

**(f) If X and Y are independent, then show that for any function h and g: $E[g(X)h(Y)] = E[g(X)]E[h(Y)]$**
**Solution:**
Assume X and Y are continuous random variables with joint density $f(x,y)$. Since they are independent, $f(x,y) = f_X(x)f_Y(y)$.
$$E[g(X)h(Y)] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x)h(y) f(x,y) dx dy = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x)h(y) f_X(x)f_Y(y) dx dy$$
Separating the integrals:
$$= \left( \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x)f_X(x) dx \right) \left( \int_{-\infty}^\infty h(y)f_Y(y) dy \right) = E[g(X)] E[h(Y)]$$
**Concepts:** [[02. Independent Random Variables]]

**(g) Prove the following: $Var(X) = E[Var(X|Y)] + Var(E[X|Y])$**
**Solution:**
By definition, $Var(X|Y) = E[X^2|Y] - (E[X|Y])^2$.
Taking the expectation of both sides:
$E[Var(X|Y)] = E[E[X^2|Y]] - E[(E[X|Y])^2]$
Using the law of total expectation, $E[E[X^2|Y]] = E[X^2]$, so:
$E[Var(X|Y)] = E[X^2] - E[(E[X|Y])^2]$  (Equation 1)
Now, consider $Var(E[X|Y])$. Since $E[X|Y]$ is a random variable, its variance is:
$Var(E[X|Y]) = E[(E[X|Y])^2] - (E[E[X|Y]])^2$
Using the law of total expectation again, $E[E[X|Y]] = E[X]$, so:
$Var(E[X|Y]) = E[(E[X|Y])^2] - (E[X])^2$   (Equation 2)
Adding Equation 1 and Equation 2:
$E[Var(X|Y)] + Var(E[X|Y]) = \left( E[X^2] - E[(E[X|Y])^2] \right) + \left( E[(E[X|Y])^2] - (E[X])^2 \right)$
$= E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = Var(X)$.
**Concepts:** [[05. Expectation and Variance by Conditioning]]

**(h) Coming home from work, Neha always encounters traffic signal. The probability that she makes it through a traffic signal is 0.2. How many traffic signals can she expect to hit before making it through one? What is the probability of the third traffic light being the first one that is green?**
**Solution:**
This follows a Geometric Distribution where success is making it through (green light), $p = 0.2$. Let $X$ be the number of trials until the first success.
1. **Expected number of signals to hit before making it through:** The expected number of failures before the first success is $E[Y] = \frac{1-p}{p} = \frac{0.8}{0.2} = 4$. She expects to wait at $4$ signals before making it through the $5^{\text{th}}$ one.
2. **Probability of the third traffic light being the first green:**
$P(X = 3) = (1-p)^{3-1} p = (0.8)^2 (0.2) = 0.64 \times 0.2 = 0.128$
**Concepts:** [[02. Discrete Random Variables]]

### Question 2

**(a) What is Normal Random Variable? Drive the formula of the following: (i) Normal density function (ii) Cumulative distribution function of Normal Random Variable.**
**Solution:**
A **Normal Random Variable** is a continuous random variable whose probability distribution is symmetric and bell-shaped, completely determined by its mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$.
**(i) Normal density function:** The PDF is given by:
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \right)^2}$$
for $-\infty < x < \infty$. (This formula is a definition and derived from the limit of a binomial distribution or the central limit theorem).
**(ii) Cumulative distribution function (CDF):**
The CDF $F(x)$ is the integral of the PDF:
$$F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{t-\mu}{\sigma} \right)^2} dt$$
Since this integral has no closed-form elementary expression, it is often expressed in terms of the standard normal CDF $\Phi(z)$:
$$F(x) = \Phi\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}} e^{-z^2/2} dz$$
**Concepts:** [[03. Continuous Random Variables]]

**(b) Let X and Y be two jointly continuous random variables with joint Probability Density Function: $f(x,y) = x + cy^2$ for $0 \le x \le 1, 0 \le y \le 1$, otherwise 0. Find the constant c.**
**Solution:**
The total volume under the joint PDF must equal 1:
$$\int_0^1 \int_0^1 (x + cy^2) dx dy = 1$$
First, integrate with respect to $x$:
$$\int_0^1 \left[ \frac{x^2}{2} + cy^2 x \right]_{x=0}^{x=1} dy = \int_0^1 \left( \frac{1}{2} + cy^2 \right) dy$$
Now, integrate with respect to $y$:
$$\left[ \frac{y}{2} + \frac{cy^3}{3} \right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{c}{3}$$
Set this equal to 1:
$$\frac{1}{2} + \frac{c}{3} = 1 \implies \frac{c}{3} = \frac{1}{2} \implies c = \frac{3}{2}$$
**Concepts:** [[01. Joint Distribution Functions]]

**(c) A fair die is rolled. Consider the following events: $A=\{1,3,5\}, B=\{2,3\}$, and $C=\{2,3,4,5\}$. Find (i) P(A|B) and P(B|A) (ii) P(A $\cup$ B | C)**
**Solution:**
Sample space $S = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$, so $P(\{x\}) = 1/6$.
$P(A) = 3/6 = 1/2$, $P(B) = 2/6 = 1/3$, $P(C) = 4/6 = 2/3$.
**(i) P(A|B) and P(B|A):**
$A \cap B = \{3\}$, so $P(A \cap B) = 1/6$.
$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{1/6}{1/3} = \frac{1}{2}$.
$P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)} = \frac{1/6}{1/2} = \frac{1}{3}$.
**(ii) P(A $\cup$ B | C):**
$A \cup B = \{1, 2, 3, 5\}$.
$(A \cup B) \cap C = \{1, 2, 3, 5\} \cap \{2, 3, 4, 5\} = \{2, 3, 5\}$.
$P((A \cup B) \cap C) = 3/6 = 1/2$.
$P(A \cup B | C) = \frac{P((A \cup B) \cap C)}{P(C)} = \frac{1/2}{2/3} = \frac{3}{4}$.
**Concepts:** [[03. Conditional Probability]]

### Question 3

**(a) Two urns contain 2 red, 3 white, and 3 red, 5 white balls, respectively. One ball is drawn at random from the first urn and transferred into the second one. A ball is then drawn from the second urn and it turns out that the ball is red. What will be the probability that the transferred ball was white?**
**Solution:**
Let $T_W$ be the event a white ball is transferred, and $T_R$ be a red ball is transferred.
$P(T_W) = \frac{3}{5}$, $P(T_R) = \frac{2}{5}$.
Let $R_2$ be the event a red ball is drawn from urn 2.
If $T_W$ occurs, urn 2 has 3 red and 6 white balls (total 9). $P(R_2 | T_W) = \frac{3}{9} = \frac{1}{3}$.
If $T_R$ occurs, urn 2 has 4 red and 5 white balls (total 9). $P(R_2 | T_R) = \frac{4}{9}$.
Using Bayes' Theorem:
$$P(T_W | R_2) = \frac{P(R_2 | T_W) P(T_W)}{P(R_2 | T_W) P(T_W) + P(R_2 | T_R) P(T_R)}$$
$$= \frac{(1/3)(3/5)}{(1/3)(3/5) + (4/9)(2/5)} = \frac{1/5}{1/5 + 8/45} = \frac{9/45}{9/45 + 8/45} = \frac{9}{17}$$
**Concepts:** [[05. Bayes' Formula and Law of Total Probability]]

**(b) Calculate E[X] when X is a Poisson random variable with parameter $\lambda$.**
**Solution:**
The PMF is $P(X=k) = e^{-\lambda} \frac{\lambda^k}{k!}$.
$$E[X] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty k \cdot e^{-\lambda} \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty e^{-\lambda} \frac{\lambda^k}{(k-1)!} = \lambda e^{-\lambda} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{\lambda^{k-1}}{(k-1)!}$$
Let $j = k-1$. Then the sum is $\sum_{j=0}^\infty \frac{\lambda^j}{j!} = e^\lambda$.
$$E[X] = \lambda e^{-\lambda} e^\lambda = \lambda$$
**Concepts:** [[04. Expectation and Variance]]

**(c) Suppose that whether or not it rains today depends on previous weather conditions through the last two days... (Same setup as PYQ 1235 Q7c)**
**(i) Transform this process into a Markov chain. (ii) How many states will it have after transformation? (iii) Provide the transition probability matrix for the same.**
**Solution:**
(i) To satisfy the Markov property, the state must encode the weather of the last two days: $(\text{Weather}_{t-1}, \text{Weather}_t)$. Let R = Rain, NR = Not Rain.
(ii) It will have **4 states**: 0: (R,R), 1: (NR,R), 2: (R,NR), 3: (NR,NR).
(iii) The transition matrix $P$:
$$P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0 & 0.3 & 0 \\ 0.5 & 0 & 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.4 & 0 & 0.6 \\ 0 & 0.2 & 0 & 0.8 \end{bmatrix}$$
Where rows and columns correspond to (R,R), (NR,R), (R,NR), (NR,NR).

### Question 4

**(a) If X is uniformly distributed over (0,10), calculate the probability that:**
**(i) X < 3  (ii) X > 7 (iii) 1 < X < 6**
**Solution:**
The PDF is $f(x) = \frac{1}{10-0} = \frac{1}{10}$ for $0 < x < 10$.
(i) $P(X < 3) = \int_0^3 \frac{1}{10} dx = \frac{3}{10} = 0.3$
(ii) $P(X > 7) = \int_7^{10} \frac{1}{10} dx = \frac{10-7}{10} = \frac{3}{10} = 0.3$
(iii) $P(1 < X < 6) = \int_1^6 \frac{1}{10} dx = \frac{6-1}{10} = \frac{5}{10} = 0.5$
**Concepts:** [[03. Continuous Random Variables]]

**(b) Let's say that 80% of all business start-ups in the IT industry report that they generate a profit in their first year. If a sample of 10 new IT business start-ups is selected, find the probability that exactly seven will generate a profit in their first year.**
**Solution:**
This is a Binomial distribution problem with $n=10$ and $p=0.8$. Let $X$ be the number of profitable start-ups.
$$P(X=7) = \binom{10}{7} p^7 (1-p)^{10-7} = \frac{10!}{7!3!} (0.8)^7 (0.2)^3$$
$$= 120 \times 0.2097152 \times 0.008 = 120 \times 0.0016777216 \approx 0.2013$$
**Concepts:** [[02. Discrete Random Variables]]

**(c) A deck of 52 playing cards, containing all 4 aces, is randomly divided into 4 piles of 13 cards each. Define events $E_1, E_2, E_3$ and $E_4$ as follows: $E_1$= {the first pile has exactly 1 ace}, $E_2$= {the second pile has exactly 1 ace}, $E_3$= {the third pile has exactly 1 ace}, $E_4$= {the fourth pile has exactly 1 ace}. Find the probability that each pile has exactly one ace.**
**Solution:**
We want to find $P(E_1 \cap E_2 \cap E_3 \cap E_4)$.
The total number of ways to divide 52 cards into 4 piles of 13 is $\frac{52!}{13!13!13!13!}$.
The number of ways to place exactly 1 ace in each of the 4 piles:
First, distribute the 4 aces among the 4 piles: $4!$ ways.
Then, distribute the remaining 48 non-ace cards into the 4 piles of 12 each: $\frac{48!}{12!12!12!12!}$ ways.
The probability is:
$$\frac{4! \times \frac{48!}{12!12!12!12!}}{\frac{52!}{13!13!13!13!}} = \frac{4! \times 48! \times 13!^4}{52! \times 12!^4} = 4! \times \frac{13^4}{52 \times 51 \times 50 \times 49}$$
$$= 24 \times \frac{28561}{6497400} \approx 0.1055$$
**Concepts:** [[01. Sample Space and Events]]

### Question 5

**(a) Suppose we know that the number of items produced in a factory during a week is a random variable with mean 500.**
**(i) What can be said about the probability that this week's production will be at least 1000?**
**Solution:** Since production is non-negative, we use Markov's inequality:
$$P(X \ge 1000) \le \frac{E[X]}{1000} = \frac{500}{1000} = \frac{1}{2}$$
**(ii) If the variance of a week's production is known to equal 100, then what can be said about the probability that this week's production will be between 400 and 600?**
**Solution:** We want $P(400 < X < 600) = P(|X - 500| < 100) = 1 - P(|X - 500| \ge 100)$.
Using Chebyshev's inequality:
$$P(|X - 500| \ge 100) \le \frac{Var(X)}{100^2} = \frac{100}{10000} = 0.01$$
Therefore, $P(|X - 500| < 100) \ge 1 - 0.01 = 0.99$.
**Concepts:** [[05. Limit Theorems and Inequalities]]

**(b) Given two random variables X and Y, define covariance of X and Y. State any three properties of covariance.**
**Solution:**
**Definition:** The covariance measures the joint variability of two random variables.
$$\text{Cov}(X,Y) = E[(X-E[X])(Y-E[Y])] = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]$$
**Properties:**
1. $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \text{Cov}(Y, X)$
2. $\text{Cov}(aX + b, cY + d) = ac\text{Cov}(X, Y)$
3. $\text{Cov}(X, X) = \text{Var}(X)$
**Concepts:** [[03. Covariance and Sums]]

**(c) Any item produced by a certain machine will be defective with probability 0.1, independently of any other item. What is the probability that in a sample of three items, at most one will be defective?**
**Solution:**
Let $X$ be the number of defective items in the sample. $X$ follows a Binomial distribution with $n=3$ and $p=0.1$.
We want $P(X \le 1) = P(X=0) + P(X=1)$.
$P(X=0) = \binom{3}{0} (0.1)^0 (0.9)^3 = 1 \times 1 \times 0.729 = 0.729$
$P(X=1) = \binom{3}{1} (0.1)^1 (0.9)^2 = 3 \times 0.1 \times 0.81 = 0.243$
$P(X \le 1) = 0.729 + 0.243 = 0.972$.
**Concepts:** [[02. Discrete Random Variables]]

### Question 6

**(a) Name and prove the following statement: if X is a random variable that takes only nonnegative values, then show that for any value a > 0, $P\{X \ge a\} \le \frac{E[X]}{a}$.**
**Solution:**
This is **Markov's Inequality**.
**Proof:** For a continuous non-negative random variable $X$ with PDF $f(x)$:
$$E[X] = \int_0^\infty x f(x) dx = \int_0^a x f(x) dx + \int_a^\infty x f(x) dx$$
Since $X \ge 0$, the first integral $\int_0^a x f(x) dx \ge 0$. Therefore:
$$E[X] \ge \int_a^\infty x f(x) dx$$
In the region where $x \ge a$, we can replace $x$ with $a$ to make the integral smaller:
$$E[X] \ge \int_a^\infty a f(x) dx = a \int_a^\infty f(x) dx = a P(X \ge a)$$
Dividing by $a$ (since $a>0$) gives: $P(X \ge a) \le \frac{E[X]}{a}$.
**Concepts:** [[05. Limit Theorems and Inequalities]]

**(b) Suppose the joint density function of X and Y is given by $f(x,y) = 6xy(2-x-y)$ for $0 < x < 1, 0 < y < 1$, otherwise 0. Compute the conditional expectation of X given that Y = y, where $0 < y < 1$.**
**Solution:**
(This is identical to PYQ 1235 Q2(a)).
$f_Y(y) = 4y - 3y^2$.
$f_{X|Y}(x|y) = \frac{6x(2-x-y)}{4-3y}$.
$$E[X|Y=y] = \int_0^1 x f_{X|Y}(x|y) dx = \frac{5-4y}{2(4-3y)}$$
**Concepts:** [[04. Conditional Expectation and Distributions]]

**(c) Suppose that 5 percent of men and 0.25 percent of women are colour-blind. A colour-blind person is chosen at random. What is the probability of this person being male? Assume that there are an equal number of males and females.**
**Solution:**
(This is identical to PYQ 1235 Q6(b)).
Let $M$ be male, $F$ be female, $C$ be colour-blind. $P(M)=0.5, P(F)=0.5, P(C|M)=0.05, P(C|F)=0.0025$.
$$P(M|C) = \frac{P(C|M)P(M)}{P(C|M)P(M) + P(C|F)P(F)} = \frac{0.05 \times 0.5}{0.05 \times 0.5 + 0.0025 \times 0.5} = \frac{0.05}{0.0525} = \frac{20}{21}$$
**Concepts:** [[05. Bayes' Formula and Law of Total Probability]]

### Question 7

**(a) Explain the n-step transition probabilities of a Markov chain using Chapman Kolmogorov equations.**
**Solution:**
(Identical to PYQ 1235 Q4(a)).
The $n$-step transition probability $P_{ij}^{(n)}$ is the probability that a process presently in state $i$ will be in state $j$ after exactly $n$ transitions.
The Chapman-Kolmogorov equations state: $P_{ij}^{(n+m)} = \sum_{k} P_{ik}^{(n)} P_{kj}^{(m)}$
This means the probability of going from $i$ to $j$ in $n+m$ steps is the sum over all possible intermediate states $k$ of the probability of going from $i$ to $k$ in $n$ steps and from $k$ to $j$ in $m$ steps.
**Concepts:** [[02. Chapman-Kolmogorov Equations]]

**(b) A company pays dividends on a monthly basis... (Same as PYQ 1235 Q4(b))**
**(i) What is the one-step transitional probability matrix for the above problem?**
**(ii) What will be the probability that dividend is paid in March 2023, given dividend is suspended in January 2023?**
**Solution:**
Let state 0 be "Paid" and state 1 be "Suspended".
(i) $P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.9 & 0.1 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix}$
(ii) We need $P_{10}^{(2)}$ (2-step transition from state 1 to state 0).
$$P^{(2)} = P^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.9 & 0.1 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix}^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.85 & 0.15 \\ 0.60 & 0.40 \end{bmatrix}$$
The probability is $P_{10}^{(2)} = 0.60$.
**Concepts:** [[02. Chapman-Kolmogorov Equations]]
