# 01. Joint Distribution Functions (Section 2.5.1)

Until now, we have looked at one random variable in isolation (e.g. your score in a game). But in the real world, multiple random variables exist at once and interact with each other (e.g., your score, the time you took to play, and the level of difficulty). 

To analyze these multiple variables simultaneously, we use **Joint Distributions**.

---

## 1. The Core Analogy: The "2D Probability Map"

> 💡 **Mental Model**: Imagine a standard map of a city. 
> - A single random variable is like a 1D street line (representing only longitude). 
> - A **Joint Distribution** is like a **2D map** (representing both latitude ($X$) and longitude ($Y$)). 
> 
> Instead of placing probability weights on a single ruler, we distribute probability "mass" across a flat 2D grid. The height of the terrain at coordinates $(x, y)$ represents how likely that specific combination is.

```
       Y (Longitude)
       ▲
       │     [x] <-- Joint Probability mass at coordinates (x,y)
       │
   ────┼──────────────► X (Latitude)
```

---

## 2. Joint CDF and recovering Marginals

> **Definition**: The **Joint Cumulative Distribution Function (Joint CDF)** of two random variables $X$ and $Y$, written as $F(a, b)$, is the probability that $X$ is less than or equal to $a$ **AND** $Y$ is less than or equal to $b$:
> $$F(a, b) = P(X \le a, Y \le b) \quad \text{for } -\infty < a, b < \infty$$

### Recovering individual distributions ("Marginals")
What if you have the 2D map ($F(a, b)$) but only care about the distribution of $X$? 
You simply let $Y$ be anything at all (which mathematically is letting $Y$ approach $\infty$):
$$F_X(a) = P(X \le a) = P(X \le a, Y < \infty) = F(a, \infty)$$
Similarly, the marginal distribution of $Y$ is:
$$F_Y(b) = P(Y \le b) = F(\infty, b)$$

---

## 3. Discrete Joint Distributions: Joint PMF

If both $X$ and $Y$ are discrete variables:

> **Definition**: The **joint probability mass function** $p(x, y)$ is:
> $$p(x, y) = P(X = x, Y = y)$$

### Recovering Marginal PMFs (Summing across columns/rows)
To find the probability that $X = x$, we sum the joint probabilities across all possible values of $Y$:
$$p_X(x) = \sum_{y: p(x,y)>0} p(x, y)$$
* **Intuition**: If you want to know the probability that a student is 20 years old, you sum the probabilities of "20-year-old freshmen", "20-year-old sophomores", "20-year-old juniors", and so on.

---

## 4. Jointly Continuous Distributions: Joint PDF

If $X$ and $Y$ take on a continuum of values, they are jointly continuous:

> **Definition**: The **joint probability density function (joint PDF)** $f(x, y)$ satisfies:
> $$P(X \in A, Y \in B) = \int_B \int_A f(x, y) \, dx \, dy$$

The Joint CDF is the double integral of the density:
$$F(a, b) = \int_{-\infty}^a \int_{-\infty}^b f(x, y) \, dy \, dx$$
Differentiating both sides gives the density:
$$\frac{d^2}{da \, db} F(a, b) = f(a, b)$$

### Recovering Marginal PDFs (Integrating out the other variable)
To find the individual density of $X$, we integrate out $Y$:
$$f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x, y) \, dy$$
* **Intuition**: To find the density at coordinate $x$ on a 1D line, we "squash" the 2D density map along the $y$-axis by accumulating the density over the entire column at $x$.

## 5. Expectation of Two Variables & Linearity

For a function of two random variables $g(X, Y)$, the expected value is:
* **Discrete**: $E[g(X,Y)] = \sum_y \sum_x g(x,y) p(x,y)$
* **Continuous**: $E[g(X,Y)] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x,y) f(x,y) \, dx \, dy$

### Linearity of Expectation: $E[aX + bY] = aE[X] + bE[Y]$
One of the most powerful rules in probability is that the expected value of a sum is the sum of expected values, **even if the variables are dependent**.

#### 📝 Proof (Continuous Case):
Let's prove this for $g(X,Y) = aX + bY$:
$$E[aX + bY] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty (ax + by) f(x,y) \, dx \, dy$$
Split the double integral into two parts:
$$E[aX + bY] = a \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty x f(x,y) \, dx \, dy + b \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty y f(x,y) \, dx \, dy$$
Rearrange terms:
$$E[aX + bY] = a \int_{-\infty}^\infty x \left( \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x,y) \, dy \right) dx + b \int_{-\infty}^\infty y \left( \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x,y) \, dx \right) dy$$
Note that $\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x,y) \, dy = f_X(x)$ and $\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x,y) \, dx = f_Y(y)$ (marginal PDFs):
$$E[aX + bY] = a \int_{-\infty}^\infty x f_X(x) \, dx + b \int_{-\infty}^\infty y f_Y(y) \, dy = a E[X] + b E[Y] \quad \blacksquare$$

## 6. Real-World Walkthroughs

### Example A: Rolling Three Dice (Example 2.29)
* **Problem**: What is the expected sum when you roll three fair dice?
* **Solution**:
  - Let $X_i$ be the outcome of die $i$ (for $i=1, 2, 3$).
  - We want the expectation of the sum: $E[X] = E[X_1 + X_2 + X_3]$.
  - The expected outcome of a single fair die is $E[X_i] = \frac{7}{2}$ (which we proved in Unit 2).
  - By linearity of expectation:
    $$E[X] = E[X_1] + E[X_2] + E[X_3] = \frac{7}{2} + \frac{7}{2} + \frac{7}{2} = \frac{21}{2} = 10.5$$

### Example B: The Matching Hats Party Expectation (Example 2.31)
* **Problem**: At a party, $N$ men mix up their hats and randomly select one each. Find the expected number of men who select their own hats.
* **Concept Breakdown**:
  1. Let $X$ be the total number of men who get their own hats. We want to find $E[X]$.
  2. We define an **indicator random variable** $X_i$ for each man $i$ ($i = 1, \dots, N$):
     $$X_i = \begin{cases} 
     1, & \text{if man } i \text{ gets his own hat} \\
     0, & \text{otherwise}
     \end{cases}$$
  3. The total number of correct selections is the sum of these indicators:
     $$X = X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_N$$
  4. Find the expectation of a single indicator $E[X_i]$:
     - Since man $i$ is equally likely to pick any of the $N$ hats:
       $$P(X_i = 1) = \frac{1}{N}$$
     - Expectations of indicators are simply their success probabilities:
       $$E[X_i] = 1 \cdot P(X_i = 1) + 0 \cdot P(X_i = 0) = \frac{1}{N}$$
  5. Apply the linearity of expectation:
     $$E[X] = E[X_1] + E[X_2] + \dots + E[X_N] = \underbrace{\frac{1}{N} + \frac{1}{N} + \dots + \frac{1}{N}}_{N \text{ times}} = N \left(\frac{1}{N}\right) = 1$$
* **Conclusion**: No matter how many people are at the party (whether 10 or 10 million), on average, **exactly one person** will get their own hat!
