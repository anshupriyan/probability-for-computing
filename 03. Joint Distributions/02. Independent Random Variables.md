# 02. Independent Random Variables (Section 2.5.2)

When multiple random variables are independent, their joint behavior becomes much simpler. Let's look at how independence is defined in 2D space and how it lets us compute sums and products easily.

---

## 1. The Core Analogy: Grid Multiplication

> 💡 **Mental Model: The Cross-Product**
> Imagine two independent spinners: Spinner $X$ and Spinner $Y$. 
> - If $X$ lands on a specific section with probability $P_X(x)$ and $Y$ lands on a specific section with probability $P_Y(y)$.
> - The probability that they land on both coordinates $(x, y)$ is simply the product of their individual probabilities. It behaves like a grid where we cross-multiply rows and columns.

### Formal Definition:
Random variables $X$ and $Y$ are **independent** if, for all real numbers $a$ and $b$:
$$P(X \le a, Y \le b) = P(X \le a) P(Y \le b) \implies F(a, b) = F_X(a) F_Y(b)$$

### Checking Independence:
* **Discrete Check**: $p(x, y) = p_X(x) p_Y(y)$
* **Continuous Check**: $f(x, y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y)$

---

## 2. Product Expectation: $E[XY] = E[X]E[Y]$

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, the expected value of their product is the product of their expected values.

#### 📝 Proof (Continuous Case):
$$E[XY] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty x y f(x, y) \, dx \, dy$$
Using the independence check ($f(x,y) = f_X(x)f_Y(y)$):
$$E[XY] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty x y f_X(x) f_Y(y) \, dx \, dy$$
Separate the integrals:
$$E[XY] = \left( \int_{-\infty}^\infty x f_X(x) \, dx \right) \left( \int_{-\infty}^\infty y f_Y(y) \, dy \right) = E[X] E[Y] \quad \blacksquare$$

## 3. Sums of Independent Variables (Convolutions)

Suppose we have two independent variables $X$ and $Y$, and we want to find the distribution of their sum: $X + Y$.

### A. Sum of Two Independent Uniforms (Example 2.36)
Let $X$ and $Y$ be independent, both uniformly distributed on $(0, 1)$. Find the density of $S = X + Y$.

1. **Write the CDF of the Sum**:
   $$F_{X+Y}(a) = P(X + Y \le a) = \iint_{x+y \le a} f(x)g(y) \, dx \, dy$$
   Since $f(x) = g(y) = 1$ for $0 < x, y < 1$, this double integral is simply the area of the intersection of $X+Y \le a$ with the unit square.
2. **Taking the Derivative (Convolution Formula)**:
   We can express the density $f_{X+Y}(a)$ as the convolution integral:
   $$f_{X+Y}(a) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(a-y) g(y) \, dy$$
3. Since $g(y) = 1$ only for $0 < y < 1$, the integral limits shrink to $[0, 1]$:
   $$f_{X+Y}(a) = \int_0^1 f(a-y) \, dy$$
   - **Case 1: $0 \le a \le 1$**
     The term $a-y$ must fall between $0$ and $1$, which means $y$ must be less than $a$. Thus:
     $$f_{X+Y}(a) = \int_0^a 1 \, dy = a$$
   - **Case 2: $1 < a \le 2$**
     The term $a-y$ must be less than 1, meaning $y$ must be greater than $a-1$. Thus:
     $$f_{X+Y}(a) = \int_{a-1}^1 1 \, dy = 1 - (a-1) = 2 - a$$
4. **Conclusion**: The density is a symmetric **triangular distribution**:
   $$f_{X+Y}(a) = \begin{cases}
   a, & 0 \le a \le 1 \\
   2-a, & 1 < a \le 2 \\
   0, & \text{otherwise}
   \end{cases}$$

### B. Sum of Two Independent Poissons (Example 2.37)
Let $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1)$ and $Y \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_2)$ be independent. Find the distribution of $X + Y$.

#### 📝 Step-by-Step Proof
Since $X$ and $Y$ are discrete, the event $\{X+Y=n\}$ is the union of disjoint events $\{X=k, Y=n-k\}$ for $k=0, \dots, n$.
$$P(X + Y = n) = \sum_{k=0}^n P(X = k, Y = n - k)$$
By independence:
$$P(X + Y = n) = \sum_{k=0}^n P(X = k) P(Y = n - k)$$
Substitute the Poisson PMFs:
$$P(X + Y = n) = \sum_{k=0}^n \left( e^{-\lambda_1} \frac{\lambda_1^k}{k!} \right) \left( e^{-\lambda_2} \frac{\lambda_2^{n-k}}{(n-k)!} \right) = e^{-(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)} \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{\lambda_1^k \lambda_2^{n-k}}{k! (n-k)!}$$
Multiply and divide by $n!$:
$$P(X + Y = n) = \frac{e^{-(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)}}{n!} \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{n!}{k! (n-k)!} \lambda_1^k \lambda_2^{n-k}$$
Recognize the Binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{k}$:
$$P(X + Y = n) = \frac{e^{-(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)}}{n!} \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} \lambda_1^k \lambda_2^{n-k}$$
By the Binomial Theorem, the sum is simply $(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)^n$:
$$P(X + Y = n) = e^{-(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)} \frac{(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)^n}{n!} \quad \blacksquare$$
* **Conclusion**: The sum of two independent Poisson variables is itself a Poisson variable with parameter $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2$.

## 4. Order Statistics (Example 2.38)

Suppose you buy $n$ light bulbs, and their lifetimes $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) with CDF $F$ and PDF $f$.
* **The Concept**: We sort the lifetimes in increasing order:
  $$X_{(1)} \le X_{(2)} \le \dots \le X_{(n)}$$
  These sorted values are called **order statistics**.
  - $X_{(1)}$ is the minimum value (the time when the first bulb fails).
  - $X_{(n)}$ is the maximum value (the time when the last bulb fails).

Let's find the CDF of the $i$-th order statistic $X_{(i)}$:
1. The event that $X_{(i)} \le x$ occurs if and only if **at least $i$** of the random variables $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are less than or equal to $x$.
2. Think of each variable $X_k \le x$ as a "success" with probability $p = F(x)$.
3. The number of successes out of $n$ trials follows a **Binomial distribution**.
4. The probability of getting at least $i$ successes is:
   $$P(X_{(i)} \le x) = \sum_{k=i}^n \binom{n}{k} [F(x)]^k [1 - F(x)]^{n-k}$$
   This is the CDF of the $i$-th order statistic.
