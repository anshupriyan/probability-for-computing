# 05. Expectation and Variance by Conditioning (Section 3.4 & 3.4.1)

Sometimes we need to compute the expected value of a variable $X$, but the probability distribution is highly dependent on another variable $Y$. In these cases, we can **condition** on $Y$ to solve the problem using a "divide and conquer" approach.

---

## 1. The Law of Total Expectation (The "Weighted Branch" Analogy)

> 💡 **Mental Model**: Imagine you are playing a game with different branches.
> - Branch 1: You win $X_1$ on average, and you choose this branch with probability $p_1$.
> - Branch 2: You win $X_2$ on average, and you choose this branch with probability $p_2$.
> 
> To find your total expected winnings $E[X]$, you simply calculate a weighted average of the branch averages:
> $$E[X] = p_1 E[X | \text{Branch 1}] + p_2 E[X | \text{Branch 2}]$$

Formally:
$$E[X] = E\left[ E[X | Y] \right]$$

#### 📝 Proof (Discrete Case):
We want to show that $E[X] = \sum_y E[X|Y=y] P(Y=y)$.
$$\begin{aligned}
\sum_y E[X|Y=y] P(Y=y) &= \sum_y \left( \sum_x x P(X=x | Y=y) \right) P(Y=y) \\
&= \sum_y \sum_x x \frac{P(X=x, Y=y)}{P(Y=y)} P(Y=y) \\
&= \sum_y \sum_x x P(X=x, Y=y) \\
&= \sum_x x \left( \sum_y P(X=x, Y=y) \right) = \sum_x x P(X=x) = E[X] \quad \blacksquare
\end{aligned}$$

## 2. Walkthrough Example: The Trapped Miner (Example 3.13)

* **Problem**: A miner is trapped in a mine containing three doors. 
  - **Door 1** leads to safety after 2 hours of travel.
  - **Door 2** leads to a tunnel that returns him to the mine after 3 hours.
  - **Door 3** leads to a tunnel that returns him to the mine after 5 hours.
  - The miner is equally likely to choose any door. What is the expected time until he reaches safety?
* **Step-by-step Walkthrough**:
  1. Let $X$ be the time until safety. We want to find $E[X]$.
  2. Let $Y$ be the door initially chosen ($Y \in \{1, 2, 3\}$), with $P(Y=i) = \frac{1}{3}$.
  3. Condition on $Y$:
     $$E[X] = \frac{1}{3} E[X|Y=1] + \frac{1}{3} E[X|Y=2] + \frac{1}{3} E[X|Y=3]$$
  4. Find the conditional expectation for each door:
     - **Door 1**: The miner escapes in 2 hours: $E[X|Y=1] = 2$.
     - **Door 2**: The miner travels for 3 hours, then finds himself back in the exact same trapped state. Since he is back at the start, his expected *additional* time to escape is just $E[X]$. Thus:
       $$E[X|Y=2] = 3 + E[X]$$
     - **Door 3**: The miner travels for 5 hours, then returns to the mine. By the same logic:
       $$E[X|Y=3] = 5 + E[X]$$
  5. Substitute these back into our equation:
     $$E[X] = \frac{1}{3}(2) + \frac{1}{3}(3 + E[X]) + \frac{1}{3}(5 + E[X])$$
  6. Solve the algebraic equation:
     $$E[X] = \frac{2 + 3 + E[X] + 5 + E[X]}{3} = \frac{10 + 2E[X]}{3}$$
     $$3 E[X] = 10 + 2 E[X] \implies E[X] = 10 \text{ hours}$$

## 3. Walkthrough Example: Quick-Sort Analysis (Example 3.16)

Quick-Sort is a classic recursive algorithm. 
- To sort $n$ values, it selects a random element (the **pivot**).
- It compares the other $n-1$ elements to the pivot, dividing them into a "smaller" group and a "larger" group.
- It then recursively sorts both groups.

What is the expected number of comparisons $M_n$ needed to sort a set of size $n$?

### 📝 Step-by-Step Logic Breakdown
1. Let the pivot we choose be the $j$-th smallest element (so there are $j-1$ smaller elements and $n-j$ larger elements). Each rank $j$ is equally likely with probability $\frac{1}{n}$.
2. If we select the $j$-th element, we must make $n-1$ comparisons against the pivot, plus recursively sort the smaller group ($M_{j-1}$) and the larger group ($M_{n-j}$):
   $$E[\text{comparisons} | \text{pivot is } j] = n - 1 + M_{j-1} + M_{n-j}$$
3. Apply the Law of Total Expectation:
   $$M_n = \sum_{j=1}^n \left( n - 1 + M_{j-1} + M_{n-j} \right) \frac{1}{n} = n - 1 + \frac{2}{n} \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} M_k$$
4. To solve this recurrence, multiply by $n$:
   $$n M_n = n(n-1) + 2 \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} M_k$$
5. Write the formula for $n+1$ and subtract to eliminate the summation term:
   $$(n+1) M_{n+1} - n M_n = 2n + 2 M_n \implies (n+1) M_{n+1} = (n+2) M_n + 2n$$
6. Diving by $(n+1)(n+2)$ and iterating the sum yields:
   $$M_n \approx 2n \ln n \approx 1.38 n \log_2 n$$
* **Conclusion**: The expected number of comparisons is $O(n \log n)$.

## 4. The Law of Total Variance

Just like expectation, we can calculate variance by conditioning on a helper variable $Y$:

> **Proposition 3.1 (Conditional Variance Formula)**:
> $$\text{Var}(X) = E\left[ \text{Var}(X|Y) \right] + \text{Var}\left( E[X|Y] \right)$$

* **Intuition**: The total uncertainty (variance) of $X$ is the sum of two parts:
  1. $E[\text{Var}(X|Y)]$: The average uncertainty of $X$ *within* each branch of $Y$.
  2. $\text{Var}(E[X|Y])$: The uncertainty *between* the branch averages of $Y$.

## 5. Walkthrough Example: Compound Variable Variance (Example 3.19)

Let $S = \sum_{i=1}^N X_i$ be a **compound random variable**, where:
- the number of terms $N$ is a random variable with mean $E[N]$ and variance $\text{Var}(N)$.
- the individual terms $X_i$ are independent and identically distributed with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, independent of $N$.
What is the variance of the sum $S$?

### 📝 Step-by-Step Walkthrough
Apply the conditional variance formula by conditioning on $N$:
$$\text{Var}(S) = E\left[ \text{Var}(S|N) \right] + \text{Var}\left( E[S|N] \right)$$

1. **Calculate conditional expectation $E[S|N=n]$**:
   If $N=n$ is fixed, the expectation of the sum of $n$ variables is $n\mu$:
   $$E[S|N=n] = n\mu \implies E[S|N] = N\mu$$
2. **Calculate conditional variance $\text{Var}(S|N=n]$**:
   If $N=n$ is fixed, the variance of the sum of $n$ independent variables is $n\sigma^2$:
   $$\text{Var}(S|N=n) = n\sigma^2 \implies \text{Var}(S|N) = N\sigma^2$$
3. **Plug these back into the conditional variance formula**:
   $$\begin{aligned}
   \text{Var}(S) &= E[N\sigma^2] + \text{Var}(N\mu) \\
   &= \sigma^2 E[N] + \mu^2 \text{Var}(N) \quad \blacksquare
   \end{aligned}$$
* **Special Case (Compound Poisson)**: If $N$ follows a Poisson distribution, then $\text{Var}(N) = E[N] = \lambda$. The variance simplifies to:
  $$\text{Var}(S) = \sigma^2 \lambda + \mu^2 \lambda = \lambda (\sigma^2 + \mu^2) = \lambda E[X^2]$$
