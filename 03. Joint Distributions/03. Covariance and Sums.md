# 03. Covariance and Sums (Section 2.5.3)

Expected values scale linearly ($E[X+Y] = E[X]+E[Y]$), but what about variances? If you buy two stocks, is the volatility (variance) of your total portfolio simply the sum of their individual volatilities? 

To answer this, we need to understand how variables move together. This is the concept of **Covariance**.

---

## 1. The Core Analogy: "Co-Movement" (Tug of War)

> 💡 **Mental Model**: Imagine two dancers.
> - **Positive Covariance**: They move in the same direction. When Dancer 1 steps right, Dancer 2 steps right. 
> - **Negative Covariance**: They move in opposite directions. When Dancer 1 steps right, Dancer 2 steps left.
> - **Zero Covariance**: They dance independently. The movement of Dancer 1 tells you nothing about where Dancer 2 is stepping.

> **Definition**: The **covariance** between $X$ and $Y$, written as $\text{Cov}(X, Y)$, is:
> $$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E\left[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])\right]$$

### The Handy Covariance Identity
$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]$$

#### 📝 Step-by-step Proof:
Let $\mu_X = E[X]$ and $\mu_Y = E[Y]$.
$$\begin{aligned}
\text{Cov}(X, Y) &= E[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)] = E[XY - \mu_Y X - \mu_X Y + \mu_X \mu_Y] \\
&= E[XY] - \mu_Y E[X] - \mu_X E[Y] + \mu_X \mu_Y \quad \text{(by linearity)} \\
&= E[XY] - \mu_Y \mu_X - \mu_X \mu_Y + \mu_X \mu_Y \\
&= E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] \quad \blacksquare
\end{aligned}$$

* **Important Note**: If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, then $E[XY] = E[X]E[Y]$, which means $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$. However, the reverse is not always true: a covariance of 0 only means there is no *linear* relationship, they could still have a non-linear dependence.

---

## 2. Correlation Coefficient ($\rho$)

Because covariance depends on the scale of your measurements (e.g. measuring height in meters vs. centimeters changes the covariance value), we normalize it.

> **Definition**: The **correlation coefficient** $\rho(X, Y)$ is:
> $$\rho(X, Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}$$
> where $\rho$ is always bounded between $-1$ and $+1$.
> - $\rho = +1$: Perfect positive linear relationship.
> - $\rho = -1$: Perfect negative linear relationship.
> - $\rho = 0$: No linear relationship.

---

## 3. Variance of a Sum

The variance of a sum of random variables includes their covariance:

> **Theorem**:
> $$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2 \text{Cov}(X, Y)$$

#### 📝 Proof:
$$\begin{aligned}
\text{Var}(X + Y) &= E\left[((X+Y) - E[X+Y])^2\right] = E\left[((X - E[X]) + (Y - E[Y]))^2\right] \\
&= E\left[(X - E[X])^2 + (Y - E[Y])^2 + 2(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])\right] \\
&= E[(X - E[X])^2] + E[(Y - E[Y])^2] + 2E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])] \\
&= \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2 \text{Cov}(X, Y) \quad \blacksquare
\end{aligned}$$

For $n$ variables, the general formula is:
$$\text{Var}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) + 2 \sum_{i < j} \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$$
* **Independence Shortcut**: If the variables are independent, all covariance terms are 0, simplifying to:
  $$\text{Var}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i)$$

## 4. Properties of the Sample Mean ($\bar{X}$)

Suppose $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are i.i.d. variables (independent, identically distributed) with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. The sample mean is defined as $\bar{X} = \frac{\sum X_i}{n}$.

* **(a) Mean of the sample mean**:
  $$E[\bar{X}] = E\left[\frac{\sum X_i}{n}\right] = \frac{1}{n} \sum E[X_i] = \frac{n\mu}{n} = \mu$$
* **(b) Variance of the sample mean**:
  Using the independence shortcut (since all $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = 0$ for $i \ne j$):
  $$\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \text{Var}\left(\frac{\sum X_i}{n}\right) = \frac{1}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) = \frac{n\sigma^2}{n^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$
* **(c) Covariance between sample mean and deviation**:
  We want to prove that $\text{Cov}(\bar{X}, X_i - \bar{X}) = 0$.
  $$\begin{aligned}
  \text{Cov}(\bar{X}, X_i - \bar{X}) &= \text{Cov}(\bar{X}, X_i) - \text{Cov}(\bar{X}, \bar{X}) \\
  &= \text{Cov}\left(\frac{1}{n} \sum_j X_j, X_i\right) - \text{Var}(\bar{X}) \\
  &= \frac{1}{n} \sum_j \text{Cov}(X_j, X_i) - \frac{\sigma^2}{n}
  \end{aligned}$$
  Since the variables are independent, $\text{Cov}(X_j, X_i) = 0$ for all $j \ne i$. The only non-zero term in the sum is when $j=i$, where $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_i) = \text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2$:
  $$\text{Cov}(\bar{X}, X_i - \bar{X}) = \frac{1}{n}(\sigma^2) - \frac{\sigma^2}{n} = 0 \quad \blacksquare$$

## 5. Indicator variable derivation: Binomial Variance (Example 2.34)

* **Problem**: A binomial variable $X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$ represents the number of successes in $n$ independent trials. Find the variance of $X$.
* **Textbook Walkthrough**:
  1. We can write $X$ as the sum of $n$ independent indicator variables:
     $$X = X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_n$$
     where $X_i = 1$ if trial $i$ is a success, and $0$ if it is a failure.
  2. Since trials are independent, the covariance between any two different trials is 0:
     $$\text{Var}(X) = \text{Var}(X_1) + \text{Var}(X_2) + \dots + \text{Var}(X_n)$$
  3. Find the variance of a single Bernoulli trial $X_i$:
     $$\text{Var}(X_i) = E[X_i^2] - (E[X_i])^2$$
     - We know $E[X_i] = p$.
     - Since $X_i$ can only be 0 or 1, $X_i^2 = X_i$ (since $0^2=0$ and $1^2=1$). Thus, $E[X_i^2] = E[X_i] = p$.
     - Substitute these in:
       $$\text{Var}(X_i) = p - p^2 = p(1-p)$$
  4. Sum the variances:
     $$\text{Var}(X) = \sum_{i=1}^n p(1-p) = np(1-p) \quad \blacksquare$$
