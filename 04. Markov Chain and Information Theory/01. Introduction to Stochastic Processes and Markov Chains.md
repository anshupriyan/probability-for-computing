# 01. Introduction to Stochastic Processes and Markov Chains (Section 4.1)

Imagine you are tracking a system that evolves over time—like the price of a stock, the weather outside, or your mood day-to-day. Since the future states are not perfectly predictable, we model these systems as **Stochastic Processes**. 

Let's build a clear intuition for these systems and explore **Markov Chains**, which have a very special "memory" property.

---

## 1. The Core Analogies

### Stochastic Process: The "Filmed Story"
> 💡 **Mental Model**: Imagine you are filming a story. 
> - A single random variable is like a single photograph (a snapshot of one moment).
> - A **Stochastic Process** is the **entire video tape**—a sequence of snapshots $\{X_t\}$ collected over a timeline $t \in T$.
> 
> The value $X_t$ is the state of the story at time $t$. If $t$ takes discrete values (like days $n = 0, 1, 2, \dots$), it is a **discrete-time process**. If $t$ is a continuous scale (like exact time $t \ge 0$), it is a **continuous-time process**. The set of all possible values $X_t$ can take is called the **state space**.

### The Markov Property: "Short-Term Memory"
> 💡 **Mental Model**: Suppose a character is wandering around a city. 
> - If the character has **Full Memory**, their choice of where to go tomorrow depends on their entire history—every street they have ever walked.
> - If the character has **No Memory**, their choice is completely independent of where they have been.
> - If the character has **Short-Term Memory** (the **Markov Property**), where they go tomorrow depends **only on where they are standing today**. The past history of how they got there is completely irrelevant.

Formally:
$$P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i, X_{n-1} = i_{n-1}, \dots, X_0 = i_0) = P_{ij}$$
* **In words**: Given the present state $X_n = i$, the conditional distribution of any future state $X_{n+1}$ is independent of the past states ($X_0, \dots, X_{n-1}$). It depends only on the current state.

---

## 2. Transition Probability Matrix ($P$)

Since the transition probability $P_{ij}$ only depends on starting in state $i$ and landing in state $j$, we collect these transition rules into a grid: the **Transition Probability Matrix ($P$)**:

$$P = \begin{bmatrix}
P_{00} & P_{01} & P_{02} & \dots \\
P_{10} & P_{11} & P_{12} & \dots \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \\
P_{i0} & P_{i1} & P_{i2} & \dots \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots
\end{bmatrix}$$

### Matrix Rules:
1. All probabilities must be non-negative: $P_{ij} \ge 0$ for all $i, j$.
2. **Row Sum Rule**: Each row must sum to exactly 1.
   $$\sum_{j=0}^\infty P_{ij} = 1 \quad \text{for } i = 0, 1, \dots$$
   * **Why**: If the system is currently in state $i$, it *must* transition to some state $j$ in the next step. The row represents a complete probability distribution for the next state.

---

## 3. Basic Walkthrough Examples

Let's see how simple transition matrices are constructed.

### Example A: Weather Forecasting (Example 4.1)
* **The Scenario**: Tomorrow's rain depends only on today's weather.
  - If it rains today, it rains tomorrow with probability $\alpha$.
  - If it does not rain today, it rains tomorrow with probability $\beta$.
* **State Definitions**: State 0 = Rain, State 1 = No Rain.
* **Transition Probabilities**:
  - Row 0 (Today is Rain): $P_{00} = \alpha$ (Rain), $P_{01} = 1 - \alpha$ (No Rain).
  - Row 1 (Today is No Rain): $P_{10} = \beta$ (Rain), $P_{11} = 1 - \beta$ (No Rain).
* **Matrix**:
  $$P = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha & 1-\alpha \\ \beta & 1-\beta \end{bmatrix}$$

### Example B: Communication System (Example 4.2)
* **The Scenario**: A digital channel transmits digits 0 and 1 through multiple stages. At each stage, there is a probability $p$ that the digit remains unchanged.
* **State Definitions**: State 0 = digit is 0, State 1 = digit is 1.
* **Transition Matrix**:
  $$P = \begin{bmatrix} p & 1-p \\ 1-p & p \end{bmatrix}$$

### Example C: Gary's Mood Transitions (Example 4.3)
* **The Scenario**: Gary's mood is cheerful (C), so-so (S), or glum (G).
  - If cheerful today, tomorrow he is C with prob 0.5, S with 0.4, G with 0.1.
  - If so-so today, tomorrow he is C with 0.3, S with 0.4, G with 0.3.
  - If glum today, tomorrow he is C with 0.2, S with 0.3, G with 0.5.
* **State Definitions**: State 0 = C, State 1 = S, State 2 = G.
* **Transition Matrix**:
  $$P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 & 0.4 & 0.1 \\ 0.3 & 0.4 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.3 & 0.5 \end{bmatrix}$$

## 4. Transforming a Process into a Markov Chain (Example 4.4)

What if a system's future depends on more than just the present? Can we still use Markov Chains? Yes, by **re-defining what a "state" means**.

* **Problem**: Suppose whether it rains tomorrow depends on the weather of **both today and yesterday**:
  - Rained past 2 days: rains tomorrow with probability $0.7$.
  - Rained today, but not yesterday: rains tomorrow with probability $0.5$.
  - Rained yesterday, but not today: rains tomorrow with probability $0.4$.
  - Dry past 2 days: rains tomorrow with probability $0.2$.
* **Analysis**: If our state only tracks "today's weather", this is not a Markov chain because we need to know yesterday's weather too.
* **Solution**: Expand the definition of a "state" to be a pair **(Yesterday, Today)**.
  - Let's define 4 states:
    - **State 0**: Rained yesterday, Rained today.
    - **State 1**: Dry yesterday, Rained today.
    - **State 2**: Rained yesterday, Dry today.
    - **State 3**: Dry yesterday, Dry today.
  - Now, let's look at transitions. If we are currently in State 0 (Rained yesterday, Rained today), what is the next state?
    - The "today" of the present state becomes the "yesterday" of the next state. So the next state must end with "yesterday was Rain" (meaning it must transition to State 0 or State 2).
    - The probability it rains tomorrow (State 0) is $0.7$. The probability it is dry tomorrow (State 2) is $1-0.7 = 0.3$.
    - Transitions to State 1 or State 3 are impossible (probability 0) because yesterday was Rain.
  - Rebuilding the transition matrix $P$ for all 4 states yields:
    $$P = \begin{bmatrix}
    0.7 & 0.0 & 0.3 & 0.0 \\
    0.5 & 0.0 & 0.5 & 0.0 \\
    0.0 & 0.4 & 0.0 & 0.6 \\
    0.0 & 0.2 & 0.0 & 0.8
    \end{bmatrix}$$
  - **Conclusion**: By bundling history into the state definition, we have transformed a non-Markovian process into a valid Markov Chain!
