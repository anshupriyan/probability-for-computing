# 02. Chapman-Kolmogorov Equations (Section 4.2)

If we know the transition probability matrix $P$, we know how a system behaves in a single step (from today to tomorrow). But what if we want to predict the future over multiple steps? If it is raining today, what is the probability it rains 4 days from now?

To calculate these multi-step transitions, we use the **Chapman-Kolmogorov Equations**.

---

## 1. The Core Analogy: Intermediate Waypoints

> 💡 **Mental Model: Flights with Layovers**
> Imagine you want to fly from City $i$ to City $j$ in two flight segments (2 steps). 
> - Since you cannot fly direct, you must have a layover at some intermediate City $k$.
> - To find the total probability of arriving at City $j$, you sum up the probabilities of all possible travel routes:
>   $$\text{Total Prob} = \sum_{\text{all Cities } k} P(\text{Fly } i \to k \text{ on segment 1}) \times P(\text{Fly } k \to j \text{ on segment 2})$$
> 
> ```
>              ┌── Layover City 1 ──┐
>              │                    │
> City i ──────┼── Layover City 2 ──┼──> City j
>              │                    │
>              └── Layover City 3 ──┘
> ```

---

## 2. The $n$-Step Transition Probability ($P_{ij}^n$)

We define $P_{ij}^n$ as the probability that a process in state $i$ will be in state $j$ after exactly $n$ additional transitions:
$$P_{ij}^n = P(X_{n+k} = j \mid X_k = i)$$

---

## 3. Deriving the Chapman-Kolmogorov Equations

The Chapman-Kolmogorov equations state that to go from $i$ to $j$ in $n+m$ steps, we can go to some intermediate state $k$ in $n$ steps, and then from $k$ to $j$ in $m$ steps, summing over all possible intermediate states $k$:

$$P_{ij}^{n+m} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P_{ik}^n P_{kj}^m$$

#### 📝 Step-by-Step Proof
1. Expand the conditional probability of going from $X_0=i$ to $X_{n+m}=j$:
   $$P_{ij}^{n+m} = P(X_{n+m} = j \mid X_0 = i)$$
2. Use the Law of Total Probability by conditioning on all possible states $k$ the system could visit at the intermediate step $n$:
   $$P_{ij}^{n+m} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P(X_{n+m} = j, X_n = k \mid X_0 = i)$$
3. Expand using conditional probability rules:
   $$P_{ij}^{n+m} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P(X_{n+m} = j \mid X_n = k, X_0 = i) P(X_n = k \mid X_0 = i)$$
4. Apply the **Markov Property**: once we know the state at step $n$ is $X_n = k$, the future state $X_{n+m}$ is independent of the past state $X_0 = i$. Thus:
   $$P(X_{n+m} = j \mid X_n = k, X_0 = i) = P(X_{n+m} = j \mid X_n = k) = P_{kj}^m$$
5. Note that $P(X_n = k \mid X_0 = i) = P_{ik}^n$. Substitute these back:
   $$P_{ij}^{n+m} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P_{ik}^n P_{kj}^m \quad \blacksquare$$

---

## 4. The Matrix Shortcut: $P^{(n)} = P^n$

If you look closely at the Chapman-Kolmogorov formula:
$$P_{ij}^{2} = \sum_k P_{ik} P_{kj}$$
This is the exact definition of **matrix multiplication**! 
If you multiply the transition matrix $P$ by itself, you get the 2-step transition matrix:
$$P^{(2)} = P \cdot P = P^2$$
By induction, the $n$-step transition probability matrix $P^{(n)}$ is simply the transition matrix $P$ raised to the power $n$:
$$P^{(n)} = P^n$$

## 5. Walkthrough Examples

### Example A: Weather Forecast over 4 Days (Example 4.8)
* **Problem**: Consider the weather Markov chain with matrix:
  $$P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix}$$
  If it rains today, what is the probability it rains 4 days from now?
* **Solution**:
  - Today is Rain (State 0). We want to find $P_{00}^4$ (row 0, column 0 of $P^4$).
  - Calculate $P^2$:
    $$P^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (0.7)(0.7)+(0.3)(0.4) & (0.7)(0.3)+(0.3)(0.6) \\ (0.4)(0.7)+(0.6)(0.4) & (0.4)(0.3)+(0.6)(0.6) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.61 & 0.39 \\ 0.52 & 0.48 \end{bmatrix}$$
  - Calculate $P^4 = P^2 \cdot P^2$:
    $$P^4 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.61 & 0.39 \\ 0.52 & 0.48 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.61 & 0.39 \\ 0.52 & 0.48 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5749 & 0.4251 \\ 0.5668 & 0.4332 \end{bmatrix}$$
  - **Conclusion**: The probability of rain 4 days from now is $P_{00}^4 = 0.5749$ (or 57.49%).

### Example B: Pensioner Capital Absorbing Barriers (Example 4.12)
* **Problem**: A pensioner starts with capital 3. Each month he spends money according to a distribution. If capital drops to 1 or less, he is broke and stays broke (absorbing state). The transition matrix for states $\{1, 2, 3\}$ is:
  $$Q = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1/2 & 1/4 & 1/4 \\ 1/4 & 1/4 & 1/2 \end{bmatrix}$$
  What is the probability that his capital ever falls to 1 or less within 4 months?
* **Solution**:
  - The starting state is 3. State 1 represents the capital being "1 or less" (broke).
  - Since state 1 is an absorbing state (if you enter it, you never leave, as shown by $Q_{11} = 1$), the probability of entering state 1 by month 4 is simply the 4-step transition probability $Q_{31}^4$.
  - Calculate $Q^2$:
    $$Q^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 5/8 & 1/8 & 1/4 \\ 3/8 & 3/16 & 7/16 \end{bmatrix}$$
  - Calculate $Q^4 = Q^2 \cdot Q^2$:
    Multiplying the matrices yields:
    $$Q_{31}^4 = \frac{201}{256} \approx 0.785$$
  - **Conclusion**: There is a 78.5% chance he goes broke within 4 months.

## 6. Unconditional Probability: The Initial State Distribution

What if we don't know the starting state with certainty? (e.g. "There is a 40% chance of rain today, and a 60% chance of no rain").

We define the **initial state distribution** as a vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$, where:
$$\alpha_i = P(X_0 = i) \quad \text{and} \quad \sum \alpha_i = 1$$

To find the probability of being in state $j$ at time $n$, we sum the conditional paths from all possible starting states:
$$P(X_n = j) = \sum_{i=0}^\infty P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i) P(X_0 = i) = \sum_{i=0}^\infty P_{ij}^n \alpha_i$$
